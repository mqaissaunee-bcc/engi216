Chapter Roadmap

A whole machine in a single scalar equation

Newton’s laws (Chapters 5–6) let you analyze a machine by drawing a free‑body diagram for every link, writing three equations apiece, and grinding through the algebra. That works, but for a one‑degree‑of‑freedom machine it is a lot of bookkeeping to answer a simple question: “how fast will it run, and how hard can it push?” This chapter offers a shortcut. The power equation wraps the entire machine — every link, spring, and damper — into one scalar energy balance.

The idea is the bank‑account idea from physics, scaled up to a full mechanism. Power in must equal the rate at which energy is going somewhere: into speeding things up (kinetic energy), into lifting weights or stretching springs (potential energy), or into heat (friction). Write that single sentence as an equation, plug in the kinematic coefficients from Chapter 4, and a machine that took dozens of force equations collapses to one line.

Think of it like…

…a household budget. You don’t need to track every coin to know whether you can afford something — you just balance money in against spending + savings. The power equation is that balance sheet for a machine: power supplied by the motor on one side; rate‑of‑change of kinetic energy (savings), potential energy (a different account), and friction (money burned) on the other. Newton’s method audits every transaction; the power equation just balances the books.

What you’ll be able to do

  • Write the energy balance of a machine and convert it to the power equation P = dT/dt + dU/dt + Pf.
  • Build the kinetic‑energy term using the equivalent inertia Ie and recognize where its rate of change dIe/dSi comes from.
  • Account for potential energy (elevation and springs) and for power dissipated by viscous and Coulomb friction.
  • Solve the inverse dynamics problem (find the unknown driver) and the forward dynamics problem (find the motion).
  • Integrate the nonlinear forward‑dynamics ODE with Euler’s method, choose a sane time step, and use a flywheel to cut speed fluctuation (the coefficient of variation Cf).
  • Compute a mechanism’s mechanical advantage Ma and relate it to efficiency η.

The 30-second picture

One sentence governs the whole chapter. Read it as a balance, left equals right:

P  =  dT/dt  +  dU/dt  +  Pf power in = speeding up + storing + heating

The orange term is motion (kinetic energy changing), the green term is storage (potential energy in height or springs), and Pf is energy lost to heat. Master those three terms and you have mastered Chapter 7.

Foundations

0 · The physics & math you’ll lean on

The power equation is built from a few ideas you already own. Before we develop it, let’s sharpen five of them. If these are second nature, skim; if not, this is the place to firm them up.

Work, energy, and power

Work is force acting through a distance; energy is stored capacity to do work; power is the rate of doing work — energy per unit time. Power is what differentiates this chapter from a static energy balance: by working with rates, the same balance that tells you “how much energy” now tells you “how fast.”

P = dW/dt = F⃗·v⃗  (force) = Tω  (torque) SI unit: watt = N·m/s = J/s
Think of it like…

…filling a pool with a hose. Total water is energy; the flow rate from the hose is power. Two pools can hold the same water (energy) but a fire hose fills one far faster (more power). When we “convert the energy balance to rates,” we’re simply switching from “how much water” to “gallons per second.”

Kinetic energy of a rigid body in planar motion

A rigid body that both translates and spins carries energy in both motions: a translational part from its mass center’s speed, and a rotational part from its spin:

T = ½ m vg² + ½ Igω² translation + rotation

Here vg is the speed of the mass center G, m is the mass, Ig is the rotational inertia about G, and ω is the angular velocity. This single expression is the seed of the entire kinetic‑energy term.

The chain rule with kinematic coefficients

This is the bridge from Chapter 4. Every body’s velocity is its kinematic coefficient times the input speed. So vg and ω are both proportional to Ṡi:

vgx = fgxṠi,   vgy = fgyṠi,   ω = h·Ṡi velocity = (geometry) × (input speed)

That substitution is what lets us pull Ṡi out of the kinetic energy and discover an equivalent inertia that depends only on geometry. Hold this thought — it pays off in §7.1.

In the real world

This is exactly how a car’s engineers reason about “rotational mass.” Heavy wheels and a heavy flywheel store kinetic energy when the car speeds up, so they feel like extra mass the engine must accelerate. The kinematic coefficients translate each spinning part’s contribution back to the crankshaft — producing one equivalent inertia the engine actually feels.

Numerical integration (a first look)

When an equation is too tangled to solve with a formula, we march forward in small time steps: “given where I am and how fast things are changing, take a tiny step and repeat.” That is the essence of Euler’s method, which we’ll use in §7.3 to solve the forward dynamics problem. You don’t need it yet — just know the spirit: small steps, repeated.

Hooke’s law for a linear spring

A linear spring resists with a force proportional to how far it is stretched from free length: Fs = Fo + k(rs−rso), where k is the stiffness. The energy stored in the spring is the area under its force–deflection line — a fact we’ll use directly for the potential‑energy term.

Section 7.1

7.1 · Developing the power equation

Start from the most basic statement of conservation of energy for a machine over some interval. Whatever net work W you put in must show up as a change in kinetic energy, a change in potential energy, or energy lost to friction:

Energy balance (Eq. 7.1):
W = ΔT + ΔU + Wf
where W = net work input, ΔT = change in kinetic energy, ΔU = change in potential energy, and Wf = energy dissipated by friction.

Now take the time derivative of every term — convert “how much energy” into “how fast.” Work‑per‑time is power, and the result is the star of the chapter:

The power equation (Eq. 7.2):
P = dT/dt + dU/dt + Pf
P = net power input, dT/dt = rate of change of kinetic energy, dU/dt = rate of change of potential energy, Pf = power dissipated by friction.

The three terms on the right — and which one wins

A machine’s personality is decided by which of the three right‑hand terms dominates. Not every machine cares about all three equally:

dT/dt — kinetic

Dominates in high‑speed, well‑lubricated machines — a turbine, a centrifuge. Most of the input goes into accelerating mass.

dU/dt — potential

Dominates in machines that lift loads or fight springs — an elevator, a hood strut, a press with a return spring.

Pf — friction

Dominates in slow, heavily‑loaded machines — a worm‑gear winch, a screw jack. Most input becomes heat.

In the real world

A figure skater’s spin is almost pure dT/dt — pulling the arms in changes the equivalent inertia and the spin speeds up, with negligible friction or height change. A canal lock is almost pure dU/dt — slow, but lifting an enormous mass of water and boat. A hand‑cranked pencil sharpener is mostly Pf — you feel the grinding heat, not acceleration. Same equation; three different machines.

Over the next three subsections we develop each term into something you can actually compute from the kinematic coefficients. Then §7.2 and §7.3 put the assembled equation to work.

7.1 · First term

Rate of change of kinetic energy & the equivalent inertia

Begin with one rigid body (Fig. 7.1). Its kinetic energy combines translation of the mass center and rotation about it:

T = ½ m vg² + ½ Igω²

Now substitute the kinematic coefficients (Chapter 4): the mass‑center velocity components are fgxṠi and fgyṠi, and the angular velocity is hṠi. Because every velocity carries the same factor Ṡi, it factors out and gets squared:

Kinetic energy in coefficient form (Eq. 7.4):
T = [ ½ m(fgx²+fgy²) + ½ Igh² ] Ṡi²
Everything in the bracket is pure geometry; the only kinematics is the Ṡi² outside.

Differentiate with respect to time (the bracket itself changes as the mechanism moves, so the product rule fires). For one body you get Eq. 7.5; summed over all the bodies in a machine it becomes Eq. 7.6:

Rate of change of kinetic energy (Eq. 7.6):
dT/dt = (ΣA) ṠiS̈i + (ΣB) Ṡi³

The two sums have names worth knowing. ΣA is the equivalent inertia Ie — the single inertia the input shaft “feels” from the whole machine. And ΣB turns out to be exactly half the rate at which that equivalent inertia changes with the input:

Equivalent inertia & its rate (Eq. 7.6 cont.):
ΣA = Ie = Σ[ m(fgx²+fgy²) + Igh² ]     ΣB = ½ dIe/dSi

So the kinetic term takes the clean final form:

dT/dt = Ie ṠiS̈i + ½ (dIe/dSi) Ṡi³ looks like I·α — but with a correction
Think of it like…

…a figure skater’s spin. The first term, IeṠiS̈i, is the familiar “inertia times acceleration” — push harder, spin faster. The second term exists because the skater’s equivalent inertia changes: pulling the arms in shrinks Ie, and that very change pumps energy into the spin even with no extra push. In a linkage, Ie breathes in and out as the geometry changes through a cycle — the dIe/dSi term is the machine’s version of arms moving in and out.

In the real world

Engine designers quote an “effective rotating inertia at the crankshaft” that lumps pistons, rods, flywheel, clutch, and geartrain into one number — that is Ie. In a reciprocating engine the pistons speed up and slow twice per revolution, so Ie genuinely rises and falls within a single turn, and the dIe/dSi term is what makes a one‑cylinder engine shake while a smooth multi‑cylinder one barely does.

7.1 · Second term

Rate of change of potential energy

Two kinds of stored energy come up again and again: energy from raising weight and energy in a spring. We handle each by writing the energy, then differentiating to a rate — and, as always, factoring out Ṡi via a kinematic coefficient.

Potential energy due to elevation

From elementary physics, raising a body of mass m to height H stores Ue = mgH. Differentiate, and recognize that the rate at which the mass center climbs is itself a kinematic coefficient times the input speed. That defines the kinematic coefficient of elevation fe:

Elevation term (Eq. 7.10):
dUe/dt = mg·fe·Ṡi
where fe = dH/dSi is how fast the mass center rises per unit of input. It is found from the basic coefficients by projecting onto the “up” direction: fe = fgxcosθe + fgysinθe.
Think of it like…

…climbing stairs vs. walking a ramp. fe is the “steepness” the input feels — how much height you gain per step of input. On a flat stretch (fe = 0) the elevation term vanishes no matter how fast you go; on a steep pitch a small input buys a lot of height, and the potential‑energy term is large.

Potential energy in a spring

The energy stored in a linear spring is the area under its force–deflection line (Fig. 7.5): a rectangle of preload plus a triangle from the stiffness. Integrating gives Eq. 7.14, and differentiating that yields a strikingly clean rate:

Spring term (Eq. 7.15):
dUs/dt = Fs·fs·Ṡi
Fs is the current spring force; fs = drs/dSi (Eq. 7.16) is the kinematic coefficient of the spring — how fast the spring stretches per unit input. It is found from an additional vector loop that spans the spring (see Example 7.2).

The pattern is the same every time: a generalized force (mg for gravity, Fs for the spring) times a kinematic coefficient (fe or fs) times the input speed. Power = force × velocity, expressed in the input’s coordinates.

In the real world

A car’s hood or trunk that stays up on its own uses a gas spring sized so its Fsfs term nearly cancels the mg fe term of the lid’s weight throughout the swing — the lid feels weightless because the two potential‑energy rates balance. Example 7.2 analyzes exactly this kind of hood‑lift spring.

7.1 · Third term

Power dissipated by friction

Friction always takes the same sign — it removes energy — but it comes in two flavors that are modeled differently: viscous damping (think fluid) and Coulomb friction (think dry rubbing).

Viscous damping

A linear damper (a dashpot, Fig. 7.8) resists with a force proportional to speed: Fc = cṙc (Eq. 7.19). Power dissipated is force times speed, so it goes as speed squared:

Viscous power loss (from Eq. 7.19):
Pf = c ṙc² = c·fc²·Ṡi²
where fc = drc/dSi is the damper kinematic coefficient, found just like the spring’s.
Think of it like…

…sticking your hand out a car window. At walking speed you barely feel the air; at highway speed it shoves your arm back hard. Viscous drag grows with the square of speed, which is why a damper is gentle when the machine creeps and fierce when it races — and why a screen door closer eases shut instead of slamming.

Coulomb (dry) friction — depends on the joint

Dry friction at a joint dissipates power equal to the friction force or torque times the rubbing velocity at that joint. The exact expression depends on the joint type (these tie back to the friction analysis of Chapter 6):

Coulomb friction power loss by joint type (Eqs. 7.20–7.22)
JointPfWhat rubs
Pin jointtyx ωyxfriction torque × relative angular velocity
Pin‑in‑a‑slotfyx vcy/cx + tyx ωyxboth sliding and turning rub
Straight slidingfyx vcy/cxfriction force × relative sliding velocity
Why friction is the hard term

Unlike the KE and PE terms, the friction forces and torques aren’t known up front — they depend on the joint loads, which depend on the motion, which depends on the friction… a circular dependency. As §7.3 shows, including joint friction in a simulation requires an iterative inverse‑dynamics solve at every time step. That is why many first‑pass analyses set friction aside and add it later.

In the real world

The two friction types feel completely different in your hands. A quality door damper (viscous) gets stiffer the faster you push — you can’t slam it. A rusty hinge (Coulomb) takes a constant hard shove to break free and then drags evenly regardless of speed, often with a squeal. Designers exploit the difference: viscous dampers to absorb shock smoothly, Coulomb friction (brakes, clutches) to hold or stop with a predictable, speed‑independent force.

Section 7.2

7.2 · The power equation & the inverse dynamics problem

Recall the two faces of machine dynamics. In the inverse dynamics problem (IDP) the motion is known — you have prescribed Si, Ṡi, and S̈i — and you want the one unknown force or torque, usually the driver that must be supplying the power.

The IDP with the power equation: the unknown driver appears linearly inside P. Because everything else (the motion, hence all three right‑hand terms) is known, the power equation becomes one linear equation in one unknown — just solve for the driver.

A prime mover is whatever supplies the power to the input: an electric motor, an engine, a hydraulic cylinder, a solenoid. In the IDP you are effectively asking, “given that I want the machine to move this way, how big a prime mover do I need?”

Think of it like…

…reading a recipe backwards. Forward dynamics is “I have these ingredients (forces) — what cake comes out (motion)?” Inverse dynamics is “I want this cake (a prescribed motion) — what ingredients (driver torque) does it take?” The inverse question is usually the easier one, because once the motion is fixed, the power bookkeeping is just arithmetic.

In the real world

Sizing a motor is pure inverse dynamics. An elevator engineer specifies the desired motion profile — accelerate, cruise, decelerate — for a full car, then uses the power balance to find the peak power the hoist motor must deliver. Robot‑joint motors, conveyor drives, and CNC spindle motors are all sized the same way: prescribe the motion, solve the power equation for the driver.

Section 7.3

7.3 · The power equation & the forward dynamics problem

The forward dynamics problem (FDP) is the hard, interesting one: the driver is known and you want the resulting motion — Si as a function of time. Substitute the kinetic‑energy term (Eq. 7.6) into the power equation and you get a differential equation:

The FDP equation (Eq. 7.35):
P = (ΣA)ṠiS̈i + (ΣB)Ṡi³ + dU/dt + Pf
Solve this for S̈i and you have the acceleration at any instant. Integrate twice and you have the motion.

Two facts make this genuinely difficult. First, you need initial conditions — the starting position and speed: Si(t0) = Si0 and Ṡi(t0) = Ṡi0. Second, and more troubling, the equation contains products of Si and Ṡi (and Ṡi³), so it is nonlinear. There is no tidy formula for the answer — it must be solved numerically.

Linear vs. nonlinear — why it matters

A linear ODE has clean closed‑form solutions (sines, exponentials). The FDP equation’s coefficients (Ie, dIe/dSi) change with position, and terms multiply each other. That is the signature of a nonlinear system — powerful and realistic, but only solvable by marching forward step by step.

7.3 · Numerical solution

Euler’s method, time steps & flywheels

Euler’s method is the simplest way to march a differential equation forward in time. Its one assumption: over a small enough step Δt, the acceleration is constant. That turns calculus into arithmetic.

Euler update (Eqs. 7.36–7.39): assume S̈i = S̈i0 (constant over the step), then
ΔṠi = S̈i0Δt  ·  ΔSi = Ṡi0Δt + ½S̈i0Δt²
Ṡi = Ṡi0 + ΔṠi  ·  Si = Si0 + ΔSi

The recipe for one step: (1) solve the FDP equation for the acceleration at the current state; (2) assume it holds over Δt; (3) update velocity and position; (4) repeat. Each step uses only what you know at its start — that is what makes Euler so easy to program.

Think of it like…

…navigating by dead reckoning. A sailor without GPS notes the current heading and speed, assumes they hold for the next few minutes, plots a short straight line, then re‑checks and repeats. Each leg is a tiny straight approximation of a curved path. Shorter legs (smaller Δt) hug the true curve more faithfully; longer legs drift off. Euler’s method is dead reckoning for a differential equation.

Choosing the time step

How small should Δt be? Small enough that the “constant acceleration” lie stays believable. The textbook’s rule of thumb works on the change in the input per step, ΔSi:

  • If Si is an angle θi, keep Δθi ≤ 5° per step.
  • If Si is a length ri, keep Δri ≤ 2% of the longest link length per step.

Rather than guess Δt and check ΔSi afterward, it is cleaner to choose ΔSi and back‑solve for the matching Δt (Eq. 7.40):

Δt = [ −Ṡi0 ± √(Ṡi0² + 2S̈i0ΔSi) ] / S̈i0 solve ΔS = ṠΔt + ½S̈Δt² for Δt
The accuracy–cost trade

Halve Δt and the path follows the truth more closely — but you double the number of steps and the computation time. Every numerical analyst lives on this trade‑off. Euler is first‑order accurate, so error shrinks only in proportion to Δt; higher‑order methods (Runge–Kutta, or the modified Euler in the videos) buy more accuracy per step at more arithmetic per step.

Including joint friction: iterate each step

Friction makes the march trickier. The rubbing velocities are easy — you know them at the start of each step. The friction forces and torques are not, because they come from a force analysis that itself needs the acceleration you’re trying to find. The fix is a successive‑iteration loop within each time step:

  1. Assume the friction terms are zero (first step) or carry them over from the previous step.
  2. Solve the FDP for the acceleration from the power equation.
  3. Use that acceleration in an IDP / force analysis to compute new friction terms.
  4. Feed the updated friction back into the FDP for a refined acceleration.
  5. Repeat steps 3–4 until the friction and acceleration converge.
  6. Advance to the next time step, seeding it with the converged friction.

Step by step, with an inner convergence loop at each, you build a simulation that honestly includes joint friction.

Steady state, the coefficient of variation, and flywheels

Run an engine‑driven mechanism long enough and it settles into a repeating cycle whose speed still wobbles within each revolution — faster on the power stroke, slower against the load. Two results from the FDP examples matter in practice:

Coefficient of variation Cf quantifies that wobble:
Cf = (ωmax − ωmin) / ωavg
Smaller Cf = smoother running.
A flywheel is a cylindrical mass added to the input crank or output shaft. By raising the equivalent inertia Ie, it stores energy on the fast part of the cycle and gives it back on the slow part — cutting Cf.
Think of it like…

…a heavy potter’s wheel. You kick it once and it coasts smoothly for many seconds, smoothing your uneven kicks into steady rotation. A light wheel would lurch with every push. The flywheel’s mass is a buffer that averages out the lumpy power — exactly what it does for a one‑cylinder engine, a punch press, or a sewing machine.

In the real world

The big disc you see on an old stationary engine or behind a punch press is the flywheel. A punch press loafs along storing energy in the flywheel, then dumps a huge burst of it during the split‑second punch — letting a small motor do a big job. The same physics tames torque pulses in every piston engine, and grid‑scale flywheels even buffer power on electrical networks.

Section 7.4

7.4 · Mechanical advantage

Every machine has a driver that supplies effort and a load that resists. The driver provides either a torque (a motor) or a force (a solenoid, a hydraulic cylinder); we call it D. The load resists either the rotation of a shaft or the motion of a point; we call it L.

Mechanical advantage (Eq. 7.46):
Ma = L / D   →   L = MaD
Ma is synonymous with leverage: how big a load the driver can overcome. Its units depend on the units of L and D (it can be dimensionless, a length, or a 1/length).
Think of it like…

…a crowbar. Push down on the long end with a small force; the short end lifts a huge load. The mechanical advantage is the ratio of the load you move to the effort you spend. A bottle‑jack, a pair of bolt cutters, a nutcracker, a gear reduction — all trade speed for force, and Ma is the exchange rate.

Finding Ma with the power equation

You could find Ma by a full force analysis — but the power equation gives a slicker route. The trick: at the instant of interest, write the total power as driver power minus load power, set it to zero (an idealized, lossless, quasi‑static balance), and the input speed cancels, leaving Ma in pure kinematic‑coefficient terms.

Procedure for the mechanical advantage of a mechanism

  1. Solve the position problem; find the basic first‑order kinematic coefficients.
  2. Express the coefficients of the points of application of any driving or load forces in terms of the basic ones.
  3. Express the angular velocity of any body carrying a driving/load torque via the coefficients and the input speed Ṡi.
  4. Express the linear velocity of the points of application of any driver/load forces via their coefficients and Ṡi.
  5. Write the total power P, substituting the first‑order coefficients.
  6. Set P = 0 and solve for Ma.

Because the coefficients change as the mechanism moves, Ma is generally a function of position — and at special configurations it can swing to zero or infinity (a toggle). That tunable leverage is the whole reason linkages exist.

In the real world

A vise‑grip or toggle clamp drives its Ma toward infinity as the links line up — a modest hand force becomes a near‑unbreakable clamping force right at the locked position. Knee‑joint exercise machines, rock‑crushers, and stamping presses all park their working stroke near a high‑Ma toggle to multiply force where it counts.

Section 7.5

7.5 · Efficiency & mechanical advantage

The Ma we just computed assumed no friction — it is the ideal leverage. A real machine never reaches it: friction eats some of the driver’s effort, so the load you can actually move is smaller. That shortfall is the efficiency.

Efficiency as a ratio of mechanical advantages:
η = Ma,actual / Ma,ideal
Any inefficiency shows up as a reduction in mechanical advantage. Equivalently (Eq. 7.86), the actual load is the ideal load scaled by η:
Lactual = η Ma D

This is a beautiful unification: rather than treating “leverage” and “losses” as separate ideas, §7.5 says efficiency simply discounts the mechanical advantage. A 90%‑efficient hoist delivers 90% of its ideal lift for a given input — the missing 10% became heat in the bearings and gear teeth.

Think of it like…

…a currency exchange with a fee. The posted rate is your ideal mechanical advantage; the teller’s commission is the friction. You hand over your dollars (driver effort) and the rate says you should get a certain amount — but after the fee you walk away with η times that. Same transaction, smaller payout. The fee is unavoidable; good design just keeps it small.

In the real world

Efficiency ratings are everywhere once you look: a worm‑gear reducer might be only 50–70% efficient (and deliberately so — the losses make it self‑locking), while a quality spur‑gear set tops 98%. A car’s drivetrain, a bicycle’s chain (~95–98%), a hydraulic press — every spec sheet quotes an η that is exactly this ratio of actual to ideal mechanical advantage. Use Tool 4 above to feel how η shaves the deliverable load.

Master balance

W = ΔT + ΔU + Wf  (7.1)
P = dT/dt + dU/dt + Pf  (7.2)

Kinetic term

dT/dt = IeṠiS̈i + ½(dIe/dSi)Ṡi³
Ie = Σ[m(fgx²+fgy²)+Igh²]

Potential terms

elevation: dUe/dt = mg·fe·Ṡi (7.10)
spring: dUs/dt = Fs·fs·Ṡi (7.15)

Friction term

viscous: Pf = c fc² Ṡi²
pin: Pf = tyxωyx (7.20)
slider: Pf = fyxvcy/cx (7.22)

Forward dynamics (FDP)

solve 7.35 for S̈i; nonlinear → numerical
Euler: ΔṠ=S̈Δt; ΔS=ṠΔt+½S̈Δt²
step: Δθ≤5° or Δr≤2% of longest link

Smoothness / leverage

Cf = (ωmax−ωmin)/ωavg; flywheel ↓Cf
Ma = L/D (7.46); η = Ma,act/Ma,ideal

Reading the terms

  • dT/dt dominates in fast, low‑friction machines (turbines)
  • dU/dt dominates when lifting loads or fighting springs (elevators, struts)
  • Pf dominates in slow, heavily loaded machines (screw jacks)
  • IDP motion known → solve linearly for the driver
  • FDP driver known → integrate for the motion
  • Ma→∞ toggle position (clamps, presses); η always < 1 in reality

Objectives Recap

What you should now be able to do

  • Write a machine’s energy balance and convert it to the power equation P = dT/dt + dU/dt + Pf, and say which term dominates a given machine.
  • Assemble the kinetic term from the equivalent inertia Ie and its rate dIe/dSi.
  • Form the potential‑energy rates for elevation ( mg feṠi ) and springs ( FsfsṠi ), and the friction power for viscous and Coulomb joints.
  • Distinguish the inverse dynamics problem (solve linearly for the driver) from the forward dynamics problem (integrate for the motion).
  • Integrate the nonlinear FDP with Euler’s method, choose Δt sensibly, and iterate for joint friction.
  • Use a flywheel to cut the coefficient of variation Cf, and compute mechanical advantage Ma and its efficiency discount η.
