Chapter Roadmap

Where this chapter fits

In Chapter 2 you mastered the vector loop method for position, and you saw that taking time derivatives of those loop equations gives you velocity and acceleration. That works — but it drags time through every step, even though the velocity and acceleration of a one‑degree‑of‑freedom mechanism are really just geometry. This chapter introduces a cleaner tool: the kinematic coefficient.

A kinematic coefficient answers the question, “If I nudge the input by a tiny amount, how much does each output move?” — purely as a property of the linkage’s shape at that instant, with no clock involved. Once you have these coefficients, velocities and accelerations fall out by multiplying by the input’s speed. The same coefficients also tell you, at a glance, where a mechanism locks up (dead positions) and where an output reverses (limit positions).

Think of it like…

…the gear ratio printed on the side of a bicycle’s drivetrain. The ratio “3.2 rear turns per pedal turn” says nothing about how fast you’re pedaling. It’s a fixed geometric fact about this gear combination. Multiply it by your pedal cadence and you get wheel speed; change cadence and the ratio doesn’t care. A kinematic coefficient is exactly that idea generalized to any linkage — and unlike a bike gear, it can change as the mechanism moves through its range.

What you’ll be able to do

  • Convert position equations into velocity and acceleration systems — both the time-based way and the coefficient way.
  • Define and compute first‑order ( hk, fk ) and second‑order ( h′k, f′k ) kinematic coefficients.
  • Recognize the Jacobian as the shared coefficient matrix and reuse it for velocity, acceleration, dead, and limit analysis.
  • Locate dead positions ( |J| = 0 ) and limit positions ( a first‑order coefficient = 0 ).
  • Extend the coefficients to points of interest like a coupler point or a mass center.
  • Read a geartrain speed ratio directly off its first‑order coefficient: ns = hk.

The 30-second picture

Hold this single chain-rule identity in your head — everything in the chapter is a variation on it:

dSk/dt  =  (dSk/dSi) · (dSi/dt) output speed = (coefficient) × (input speed)

The orange factor is geometry (the kinematic coefficient). The green factor is how fast you drive the input. Separating them is the whole idea.

Foundations

0 · The math you’ll lean on

Kinematic coefficients are an exercise in careful differentiation. Before the mechanism math, let’s make sure four calculus and linear-algebra ideas are sharp. If these are second nature, skim; if not, this is the time to firm them up.

The chain rule

If a quantity y depends on x, and x depends on t, then the time rate of y is the “sensitivity” of y to x multiplied by the rate of x:

dy/dt = (dy/dx)·(dx/dt)
Think of it like…

…currency conversion through a layover. To get from dollars to yen you might go dollars → euros → yen. The total exchange rate is the product of the two leg rates. The chain rule is the same bookkeeping: each “leg” ( dy/dx and dx/dt ) is a rate, and you multiply them to convert all the way through.

The product rule (you’ll need it constantly)

Most terms in a loop equation look like r cosθ or r sinθ — a product. Differentiating a product never gives just one term:

d(uv)/dt = u̇·v + u·v̇

When you differentiate twice (for acceleration or second-order coefficients) the product rule fires repeatedly, which is exactly why the time-based route gets messy — and why a bookkeeping system helps.

Linear systems & the determinant

A 2×2 system A x = b has a unique solution as long as the matrix A is invertible — equivalently, as long as its determinant is nonzero. For

|A| = |a  b ; c  d| = ad − bc

When |A| → 0 the solution blows up. Keep that picture — it returns in §4.3 as the signature of a dead position.

Think of it like…

…two streets on a map. Two equations are two lines; their solution is the intersection. As the lines become more and more parallel, the determinant shrinks toward zero and the intersection slides off toward infinity. When the lines are exactly parallel ( determinant = 0 ) there’s no finite crossing — the system can’t be solved. A mechanism in a dead position has its “lines” parallel.

Implicit differentiation

The loop equations don’t hand you θ3 as a tidy formula of θ2; they tie the variables together implicitly, e.g. “these projections must sum to zero.” You differentiate the whole equation with respect to the input and solve for the derivative you want. That single move — differentiate the constraint, then solve for the rate — is the engine of this entire chapter.

In the real world

Every robot-arm controller and CNC machine does this conversion thousands of times per second. The motors are told a desired tool-tip velocity; the controller multiplies by the inverse of a coefficient matrix (the Jacobian) to find the joint speeds to command. The word “Jacobian” you’ll meet in §4.2 is the very same object robotics engineers stare at all day.

Section 4.1

4.1 · Time-based velocity & acceleration of the four-bar

Let’s start where Chapter 2 left off. The four-bar loop equation r2 + r3 − r4 − r1 = 0 splits into an X and a Y scalar equation. Differentiate both once with respect to time and you get the velocity equations:

−r2sinθ2 θ̇2 − r3sinθ3 θ̇3 + r4sinθ4 θ̇4 = 0 (4.3)
r2cosθ2 θ̇2 + r3cosθ3 θ̇3 − r4cosθ4 θ̇4 = 0 (4.4)

Treating θ2 (and therefore θ̇2) as the known input, group the unknowns θ̇3, θ̇4 on the left in matrix form:

[ −r3sinθ3   r4sinθ4 ;
  r3cosθ3   −r4cosθ4 ] [ θ̇3 ; θ̇4 ] = [ r2sinθ2 θ̇2 ; −r2cosθ2 θ̇2 ] (4.5)

Differentiate again — carefully applying the product rule to every term — and you get a second matrix system for the accelerations θ̈3, θ̈4 with the same matrix on the left and a right-hand side full of θ̇2 terms:

[ −r3sinθ3   r4sinθ4 ;
  r3cosθ3   −r4cosθ4 ] [ θ̈3 ; θ̈4 ] = ( terms in θ̈2, θ̇22, θ̇32, θ̇42 ) (4.8)
Notice this — it’s the whole motivation

The same 2×2 matrix appears on the left of both the velocity system (4.5) and the acceleration system (4.8). Only the right-hand side changes. That matrix is purely geometric. Time-based differentiation works, but it (a) is cumbersome — the product rule explodes the second derivative — and (b) hides the fact that the heart of the problem is geometry, not timing. Section 4.2 fixes both.

Tool 1 · The chain-rule “speedometer”

Separate the geometry from the clock

Here is the central identity made tangible. The output’s velocity is the coefficient (a fixed geometric ratio at this instant) times the input speed. Slide the input speed and watch the output speed track it — while the coefficient stays put. Then change the coefficient and watch the slope of the relationship change.

Set the coefficient to 0 and notice the output dial freezes no matter how hard you drive the input — that is a limit position (§4.4) in miniature.

Section 4.2

4.2 · Kinematic coefficients, defined

We restrict the discussion to planar, one-degree-of-freedom mechanisms. For such a mechanism there are n scalar position equations and (n + 1) unknown variables. One of those variables is the input — call it Si. The remaining n are outputs, written Sk for 1 ≤ k ≤ n. Because the mechanism has one DOF, every output is determined once you fix the input.

Kinematic coefficient. A derivative of an output variable Sk with respect to the input variable Si. The first-order coefficient is dSk/dSi; the second-order coefficient is d²Sk/dSi².

The defining feature: there is no time in these derivatives. They are properties of the mechanism’s geometry at a given configuration. The notation depends on whether the output is an angle or a length:

If Sk is a direction θk

hk = dθk/dSi
h′k = d²θk/dSi² (4.9)

If Sk is a length rk

fk = drk/dSi
f′k = d²rk/dSi² (4.10)

How to get them: differentiate the loop, keep the same matrix

Differentiate each position equation with respect to the input instead of time. Where time-based work produced θ̇k, coefficient work produces hk; where it produced θ̈k, it produces h′k. For the four-bar with input θ2, the first-order coefficients solve the system whose matrix is the Jacobian J:

J [ h3 ; h4 ] = [ r2sinθ2 ; −r2cosθ2 ] first-order

Differentiate once more to get the second-order coefficients — same Jacobian on the left, a right-hand side built from the h’s you just found:

J [ h′3 ; h′4 ] = ( terms in h3², h4² ) second-order
The payoff

You solve the position equations once (Newton’s method, Chapter 2), and that already gives you the Jacobian. Reuse it for first-order coefficients, reuse it again for second-order coefficients. One matrix, factored once, drives velocity, acceleration, dead-position, and limit-position analysis. That economy is why kinematic coefficients are the professional’s tool.

Think of it like…

…a recipe written in “parts” instead of cups. “Two parts flour to one part water” is a coefficient: it doesn’t commit to a batch size. Decide later that one part = 200 g (that’s your input speed) and every ingredient scales instantly. Kinematic coefficients are the linkage’s recipe in parts; the input speed is the batch size you pour in at the end.

Interpretation

Units & the physical meaning

What the coefficients mean

The first-order coefficient is exactly the ratio of output velocity to input velocity — the chain rule rearranged:

dSk/dSi = ( Ṡk / Ṡi ) (4.12)

The second-order coefficient is the rate of change of the first-order coefficient as the input advances — how quickly the “gear ratio” itself is shifting:

d²Sk/dSi² = (d/dSi)( dSk/dSi )
Think of it like…

…a car’s continuously variable transmission (CVT). The first-order coefficient is the instantaneous gear ratio. The second-order coefficient is how fast the CVT is changing that ratio. If you held the ratio fixed there’d be no second-order term — which is precisely why geartrains (§4.6), with constant ratios, have h′k = 0.

Units: it depends on what’s an angle and what’s a length

Because a coefficient is output-per-input, its units are (output units)÷(input units). The four cases:

A rack-and-pinion steering rack turns the steering shaft (an angle) into rack travel (a length), so its first-order coefficient carries units of length per radian — literally “millimeters of rack per radian of wheel.” That number is on the spec sheet of every steering system; it’s a kinematic coefficient by another name.

Section 4.3

4.3 · Finding dead positions

A dead position (also called a lock-up or singular position) happens when the Jacobian becomes singular:

|J| = 0 dead position

Watch what that does to the coefficients. Solving for hk divides by |J|, so as |J| → 0 the kinematic coefficients tend to infinity. Taken literally, that says a finite input velocity would produce an infinite output velocity — physically impossible. The only consistent reading is the reverse: at this configuration the input cannot move. The mechanism is “dead” with respect to that input.

Think of it like…

…pushing a door exactly along its own plane instead of perpendicular to it. No matter how hard you push, the door won’t swing — your effort has no leverage on the hinge. The math “wants” an infinite response for any motion, which is its way of telling you the input direction you chose is locked. Pick a different driver (push the door face-on) and motion is possible again.

Dead vs. limit — keep them straight

A dead position is about the input: |J| = 0, coefficients blow up, the chosen input can’t drive the linkage. A limit position (next section) is about an output: its first-order coefficient is zero, so that output momentarily stops and reverses while the input keeps going. One is the matrix going singular; the other is a single coefficient passing through zero.

Tool 4 · Determinant scope

Hunt for |J| = 0 around the cycle

This scope sweeps the crank through a full turn and plots the Jacobian determinant |J| for the four-bar you size below. Wherever the curve touches or crosses the zero line, you’ve found a dead position — the tool marks it. Drag the green cursor along the chart and watch the linkage on the left assume the corresponding posture; you’ll see the coupler and rocker line up (transmission angle → 0 or π) exactly where |J| dips to zero.

In the real world

Locking pliers (Vise-Grips) and toggle clamps deliberately park themselves at a dead position. As the handle closes, the linkage approaches |J| = 0, where it can resist enormous force with almost no input effort — that’s the satisfying “snap” and the reason the clamp holds without you holding it. Engineers exploit the same singularity that, elsewhere, they work hard to avoid.

Section 4.4

4.4 · Finding limit positions

When an output reaches the end of its travel it has to reverse direction. At the instant of reversal its velocity passes through zero. Since the output’s velocity is its first-order coefficient times the input speed,

Ṡk = hk · Ṡi

and the input is still moving ( Ṡi ≠ 0 ), the only way the output velocity can be zero is for its first-order coefficient to vanish:

hk = 0 limit position of output k

So locating a limit position is a root-find: sweep the input and find where the relevant first-order coefficient crosses zero. There the output momentarily halts — its extreme position — and then comes back.

Think of it like…

…a ball thrown straight up. At the peak its velocity is exactly zero for an instant, even though gravity (the “input”) never stopped acting. The peak is the ball’s limit position. A rocker arm at the end of its swing is doing the same thing: stationary for an instant, input still driving, about to reverse.

Tool 5 · Limit-position finder

In the real world

The oscillating-arm of a windshield wiper, a metronome, and a sewing-machine needle bar all live between two limit positions. Designers place those limits precisely — the wiper must clear the same arc every stroke, the needle must rise fully clear of the fabric. “Where does hk = 0?” is the question that sets the stroke.

Section 4.5

4.5 · Kinematic coefficients of points of interest

The coefficients we’ve found so far are the basic ones — derivatives of the loop-equation unknowns (Sk) with respect to the input. But often you care about a point that isn’t a loop variable: the mass center of a link (for dynamics, Chapter 5+), the tip of a coupler, the location of a cutting tool. Once the basic coefficients are known, you get any point’s coefficients in two steps:

  1. Write a vector locating the point of interest in the fixed frame, using the loop variables.
  2. Differentiate that vector’s X and Y components with respect to the input, applying the chain rule through the basic coefficients.

For a coupler point C referenced from the pin between links 2 and 3 by a vector r′3 at angle (θ3 + φ3), the components are:

rCx = r2cosθ2 + r′3cos(θ3+φ3)
rCy = r2sinθ2 + r′3sin(θ3+φ3) (4.37–4.38)

Differentiate once with respect to θ2 (using h3 wherever θ3 appears) to get the point’s first-order coefficients fCx, fCy; differentiate again (now h′3 appears too) for the second-order f′Cx, f′Cy. Then velocity and acceleration of C are just:

vCx = fCx·θ̇2    aCx = f′Cx·θ̇2² + fCx·θ̈2
Think of it like…

…locating a passenger on a Ferris wheel that’s mounted on a moving truck. The truck’s position is one vector; the seat’s offset from the wheel’s hub is another. Add them to locate the passenger, then differentiate the sum. The point-of-interest trick is exactly this “vector to a base, plus a vector out to the point, then differentiate the whole chain.”

In the real world

A coupler point traces those beautiful looping curves you see in mechanism design — film-advance claws in old movie projectors, mixing-blade paths, walking-robot foot trajectories. The path’s shape is set by the basic coefficients; its speed and acceleration along the path (which decide wear and vibration) come straight from fCx, fCy and their primes. Tool 6 in §Examples animates exactly this curve.

Section 4.6

4.6 · Kinematic coefficients of geartrains

Geartrains are the friendliest case. Their position equations are only rolling-contact equations (Chapter 3), which means:

  • Every scalar unknown is an angle — Sk and Si are always θk, θi.
  • The equations are linear, so the first-order coefficients hk are constants and the second-order coefficients h′k are zero.

Because the ratio never changes, the first-order coefficient simply is the speed ratio:

ns = ωk/ωi = hk (4.43, 4.45)
Tidy result

Everything that made the four-bar hard — the changing Jacobian, the nonzero second-order terms, the dead/limit positions — vanishes for a simple geartrain. The whole kinematic story is one constant number, hk, and it equals the gear speed ratio you already know from Chapter 3. Kinematic coefficients aren’t a new theory for gears; they’re the same idea, simplified.

Tool 6 · Geartrain coefficient = speed ratio

Build a train, read hk straight off it

Add meshes by choosing tooth counts. The tool multiplies the stage ratios, applies the sign for each external mesh, and reports the overall first-order coefficient hk — which is the speed ratio — plus the confirmation that h′k = 0.

Think of it like…

…the single number on a power-drill’s gearbox: “1: 0–450 rpm / 2: 0–1500 rpm.” Each setting is one fixed hk. The drill never “shifts mid-hole” on its own, so there’s no second-order coefficient — h′k = 0, exactly as the theory says for a constant-ratio train.

In the real world

An automotive final-drive ratio (say 3.73:1) is a first-order kinematic coefficient stamped on the differential. Marketing calls it a “ratio,” the parts catalog calls it “final drive,” and this chapter calls it hk — three names for the same constant geometric derivative.

Worked Examples

Worked examples

Two textbook examples, fully worked. The first is a step-through derivation you advance one logical move at a time; the second is animated so you can watch a coupler point’s velocity and acceleration come from its coefficients.

Example 4.1 · Inverted crank-slider

The mechanism is an inverted crank-slider with loop equation r1 + r2 − r3 − r4 = 0. Its X, Y components plus a geometric constraint give three position equations:

r1 + r2cosθ2 − r3cosθ3 − r4cosθ4 = 0  (4.16)
r2sinθ2 − r3sinθ3 − r4sinθ4 = 0  (4.17)
θ4 − π/2 − θ3 = 0  (4.18)

Knowns: r1, θ1 = 0°, r2, r4.  Unknowns: θ2, θ3, r3, θ4. Take θ2 as the input Si; the three outputs are θ3, r3, θ4, so the coefficients we want are h3, f3, h4 (first order) and their primes (second order).

Differentiating (4.16)–(4.18) with respect to θ2 gives three equations linear in h3, f3, h4, written with the 3×3 Jacobian J:

[ r3sinθ3   −cosθ3   r4sinθ4 ;
 −r3cosθ3   −sinθ3   −r4cosθ4 ;
 −1    0    1 ] [ h3 ; f3 ; h4 ] = [ r2sinθ2 ; −r2cosθ2 ; 0 ] (4.22)

The 3×3 matrix is the same Jacobian you’d build solving the position equations by Newton’s method — no need to recompute it. Differentiating once more produces the second-order system with the same J and a right-hand side carrying the Coriolis-type terms in 2f3h3:

J [ h′3 ; f′3 ; h′4 ] = ( terms in r2, h3², h4², and 2f3h3 ) (4.26)

With both sets of coefficients known, the time derivatives of the outputs follow by (4.11) and (4.13):

θ̇3 = h3θ̇2    ṙ3 = f3θ̇2    θ̇4 = h4θ̇2
θ̈3 = h′3θ̇2² + h3θ̈2    r̈3 = f′3θ̇2² + f3θ̈2    θ̈4 = h′4θ̇2² + h4θ̈2

  • Locate dead positions ( |J| = 0 ) and limit positions ( hk = 0 ), and explain the physical difference.
  • Extend coefficients to a point of interest (coupler point, mass center) via the vector-and-differentiate method.
  • Read a geartrain speed ratio directly off its first-order coefficient, and explain why h′k = 0 there.
