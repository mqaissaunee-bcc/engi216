Orientation

What this chapter is about

Every machine you have ever used — a pair of scissors, a car’s windshield wiper, a backhoe, a robot arm — is built from rigid pieces connected so that they move in a controlled, predictable way. This chapter gives you the vocabulary and the first analytical tools to describe any such device.

By the end of this lecture you will be able to…

  • Identify and classify the joints connecting rigid links, and count the degrees of freedom each one permits or removes.
  • Draw a skeleton diagram that captures only the geometry that affects a mechanism’s motion.
  • Distinguish a kinematic chain, a mechanism, and a machine, and generate kinematic inversions.
  • Apply Gruebler’s criterion to compute the theoretical degrees of freedom F.
  • Explain why the real mobility M can exceed F, and why F “can be fooled.”
  • Use Grashof’s criterion to predict whether a four-bar linkage gives a crank or only a rocker.
Kinematics — the study of motion that is independent of force and time. It is “purely geometric.”
Dynamics — the study of the relationship between forces/torques on a body and the time-based motion that results.
Mechanism — a device consisting of a system of interconnected rigid links.
Where dynamics is headed (preview of later chapters)

Dynamics problems come in two flavors. Knowing which one you face tells you what is given and what you must find:

Two kinds of dynamics problems
Problem typeKnownTo be determined

Chapter 1 stays entirely in the kinematic world: we ask only what motions are geometrically possible, never how much force it takes.

Section 0 · Foundational background

Degrees of freedom — the idea everything rests on

Before joints make sense, you need one concept cold: the degree of freedom (DOF) of a rigid body is the number of independent coordinates needed to fully specify its position.

A free body in a plane

A rigid body floating freely on a tabletop can do three independent things: slide in x, slide in y, and rotate by angle θ. That is 3 DOF. This “3” is the reason Gruebler’s equation starts with a factor of 3.

DOFfree, planar body = 3  (x, y, θ)

A free body in space

In three dimensions a free body has 6 DOF: three translations and three rotations. This course is almost entirely planar, so we use 3, but it is worth knowing where the spatial “6” comes from.

DOFfree, spatial body = 6
Drag a free body

Feel the three planar freedoms

Drag the body to translate it. Use the rotation slider to spin it. The readout shows the three coordinates that — together — are exactly what “3 DOF” means.

x = 300 px y = 150 px θ = 0° Independent coordinates: 3
The central trade

A joint connects two bodies and removes some of those freedoms. The whole of Gruebler’s criterion is bookkeeping: start from the freedoms all the loose links would have, then subtract what each joint takes away.

Section 1.1

Joints

Joint: a permanent contact between two links.   Link: simply a rigid body.

For planar mechanisms the set of possible joints is limited to two families, named in this course by how many freedoms they permit: P1 joints and P2 joints.

P1 joints — one freedom permitted

A P1 joint eliminates two of the relative degrees of freedom between the two links, leaving exactly one. There are three types:

  • Pin joint — relative rotation only
  • Sliding joint — relative translation only
  • Rolling joint — rolling without slipping

P2 joints — two freedoms permitted

A P2 joint eliminates only one relative degree of freedom, leaving two. There is one known type:

  • Slipping joint — simultaneous rotation and sliding (rolling with slipping). Both θ and rx are independent joint variables.
Cross-reference for other textbooks

Most references call a one-freedom contact a lower pair (surface contact: revolute = pin, prismatic = slider) and a two-freedom rolling/sliding contact a higher pair (point or line contact, e.g. a cam on a follower or meshing gear teeth). This course’s P1 = lower pair and P2 = higher pair. Same physics, different label.

Animated joint demonstrator

Pick a joint type. Link 1 (ground, hatched) is fixed; link 2 (cyan) moves with the single freedom the joint permits. Drive it with the slider or press Animate. Watch the joint variable that changes.

The multiple-pin-joint rule (it trips everyone up)

When several links are pinned together at the same axis, do not count one joint per link. The rule from the text:

number of pin joints = (links joined at that point) − 1

So three links sharing one pin form two pin joints, not one and not three. Four links sharing a pin form three joints. Forgetting this is the most common error in Gruebler calculations — we will revisit it in Section 4.

Section 1.2

Skeleton Diagrams

Skeleton diagram: a simplified drawing of a mechanism that shows only the dimensions that affect its kinematics.

A real backhoe is a mess of hydraulics, paint, and steel plate. But its motion depends only on a handful of pivot-to-pivot distances. A skeleton diagram throws away everything else: links become lines (or triangles, for ternary links), pins become small circles, the fixed link gets ground hatching, and sliders become little blocks on a guide.

The standard symbols

Why this matters

If two machines reduce to the same skeleton, they have the same kinematics — a windshield-wiper linkage and a certain locomotive drive may be kinematically identical. The skeleton is the abstraction that lets one analysis serve many machines.

Section 1.3

Mechanisms and Machines

Planar kinematic chain: a system of links and joints undergoing planar motion.
Planar mechanism: a planar kinematic chain that has one fixed link (the ground).
Machine: a mechanism used to transmit power from a source to a load.

The hierarchy in one breath

Links + joints → kinematic chain. Pin one link to ground → mechanism. Push power through it from a source to a load → machine. A bicycle’s chain-and-sprocket is a mechanism; the whole bicycle doing work on the road is a machine.

Kinematic inversion

Kinematic inversions are mechanisms derived from the same kinematic chain but with a different link fixed as ground.

Here is the elegant part: take one four-link chain, then choose which link to bolt down. Each choice is an inversion and produces a genuinely different machine — even though the chain is unchanged. The slider-crank chain alone gives the engine, the oscillating-cylinder engine, the Whitworth quick-return, and the hand pump.

Section 1.4

Gruebler’s Criterion & Degrees of Freedom

Degrees of freedom (of a mechanism): the minimum number of independent joint variables needed to completely define the mechanism’s configuration.

Now we cash in Section 0. We have N links. If they were all loose, that would be 3N planar freedoms. But one link is ground (subtract 3, so 3(N−1)), and every joint removes freedoms: each P1 removes 2, each P2 removes 1.

F = 3(N − 1) − 2P₁ − P₂ Eq. 1.2 — Gruebler
What each symbol means
SymbolMeaningWhy the coefficient
NTotal number of links (count the ground link)Each free planar link has 3 DOF
N−1Moving linksGround contributes no freedom
P1Number of P1 (one-freedom) jointsEach removes 2 freedoms
P2Number of P2 (two-freedom) jointsEach removes 1 freedom
FTheoretical degrees of freedomThe number of independent inputs needed
Reading the answer

F = 1 → one input controls the device (most useful machines).   F = 0 → a rigid structure (a truss; it cannot move).   F < 0 → statically indeterminate — over-constrained, with degree of indeterminacy equal to |F|.   F ≥ 2 → needs two or more independent inputs (e.g. a robot arm).

The same equation appears elsewhere as the Grübler–Kutzbach criterion, usually written F = 3(n−1) − 2j₁ − j₂ with j1 lower pairs and j2 higher pairs — identical to ours.

F = 1 → constrained mechanism: one input fully controls it.
Section 1.5

Mobility — when the formula lies

Gruebler’s F is a theoretical count. It assumes every link length and joint placement is “generic.” Special geometry can give a mechanism more real freedom than F predicts. The honest count is the mobility.

Mobility (M): the actual number of degrees of freedom. Always M ≥ F. Equivalently, M is the number of P1 joints you would need to remove to make the structure determinate.
M ≥ F the formula can be “fooled,” never too high

The classic counter-example: the parallelogram with a redundant link

A parallelogram four-bar has F = 1, and that is correct. But add a fifth link parallel to the coupler — geometrically redundant — and Gruebler now reports F = 0 (a structure), even though the linkage still moves happily. The real mobility is M = 1. The formula was fooled by the special parallel geometry.

Redundancy demonstrator

Toggle the redundant link

Worked logic from the text (Example 1.12 idea)

To find M directly: imagine “locking out” (removing) P1 joints one at a time until the thing becomes a rigid structure. The number you had to remove is the mobility. Lock the pin between links 1 and 2 of a four-bar and it becomes a three-link truss — a structure — so M = 1, agreeing with Gruebler here.

Section 1.6

Grashof’s Criterion

A four-bar mechanism has F = 1, so one input drives it. But what kind of motion comes out? Can the input link spin all the way around (a crank), or can it only rock back and forth (a rocker)? Grashof’s criterion answers this from the link lengths alone.

Lever — the generic term for a ground-pivoted link.
Crank — a lever capable of continuous rotation (full 360°).
Rocker — a lever that only oscillates between limit positions.

Label the four lengths: Lmax (longest), Lmin (shortest), and La, Lb (the two intermediate lengths). Grashof’s criterion:

Lmax + Lmin ≤ La + Lb Eq. 1.10 — Grashof

When this holds, the shortest link can make complete revolutions while the three longer links can only oscillate relative to one another. Which specific behavior you get depends on where the shortest link sits:

The four-bar family (Grashof classification)
ConditionShortest link is…ResultBehavior
Lmax+Lmin < La+Lb
(Grashof)		an input/output side linkCrank-rockerInput cranks 360°, output rocks
the ground linkDouble-crank (drag link)Both grounded links rotate fully
the couplerDouble-rocker (Grashof type)Both levers rock; coupler revolves
Lmax+Lmin = La+Lb(equality)Change-pointLinks can become collinear / overlap (e.g. parallelogram, deltoid)
Lmax+Lmin > La+Lb(non-Grashof)Triple-rockerNo link revolves fully; all three movers oscillate
Non-Grashof cases, in the text’s words

When Eq. 1.10 is contradicted: if the sum of the intermediate lengths is greater than the sum of shortest + longest, no link can revolve completely. If they are equal, the links can become collinear and may overlap (the change-point case).

Parametric four-bar simulator

Build a four-bar and watch Grashof decide

Set the four link lengths. The mechanism tries to drive link 2 continuously. The panel computes Lmin+Lmax vs La+Lb, names the class, and tells you live whether the crank actually completed a full turn. Try to make a crank-rocker, then lengthen the short link until it becomes a triple-rocker.

The triangle-geometry origin (why the inequality is what it is)

At the two “limit” positions of a four-bar, three of the links momentarily line up to form a triangle with the ground link. For the short link to swing all the way through, the triangle inequality must permit the links to fold and unfold past each other — and working that condition out for the worst-case position yields exactly Lmax + Lmin ≤ La + Lb. The crank-rocker in Figure 1.41 is the canonical picture: link 2 is the crank (input), link 4 the rocker (output), and the analysis of that triangle geometry is Grashof’s criterion.

Worked Examples

Examples (from the text)

Reveal each solution only after you have attempted it. Each mirrors a worked example from Chapter 1.

Example 1.1 — Degrees of freedom of a truss

Problem. Compute the theoretical degrees of freedom of the truss in Figure 1.34: two links pinned together, each also pinned to ground.

Count. Including ground, N = 3 links. The joints are all pins: two links to ground (2 pins) + the connection between the two moving links (1 pin) = P₁ = 3. No point contacts, so P₂ = 0.

F = 3(N−1) − 2P₁ − P₂ = 3(3−1) − 2(3) − 0 = 6 − 6 = 0

Interpretation. F = 0 → a rigid structure. A triangle of pinned links cannot move — exactly why trusses are built from triangles.

Example 1.3 — A statically indeterminate structure

Problem. Compute the theoretical DOF of the structure in Figure 1.36.

Count. N = 4 links and P₁ = 5 pin joints. No point contacts, so P₂ = 0.

F = 3(4−1) − 2(5) − 0 = 9 − 10 = −1

Interpretation. When F = 0 the structure is determinate. When F < 0 it is statically indeterminate, and the degree of indeterminacy equals |F|. Here |−1| = 1: the structure is over-constrained by one.

Example 1.12 — Mobility of a four-bar by lock-out

Problem. Determine the mobility of the four-bar in Figure 1.14.

Reasoning. Lock out (remove) the pin joint between links 1 and 2, fusing them into one link. The four-bar becomes a three-link truss — a structure. We had to remove exactly one P1 joint to reach a structure.

M = 1   (agrees with the F-number calculation)

Takeaway. Mobility-by-lock-out is the physical sanity check on Gruebler. When they agree, the geometry is generic; when M > F, you have found special geometry.
