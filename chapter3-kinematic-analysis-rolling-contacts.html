Chapter Roadmap

Where this chapter fits

In Chapter 2 you learned the vector loop method — draw vectors link-to-link around a closed mechanism, set their sum to zero, and solve the resulting position equations. That method handles pin joints and sliders beautifully. But it has a blind spot.

The blind spot the vector loop method has

The vector loop method is unaware that two rolling bodies in contact must turn together in a fixed relationship. It sees two gears merely as two touching circles and writes a loop equation that closes geometrically — but that equation says nothing about how fast one gear must spin when the other spins. To capture that, we must supplement the loop equations with new rolling-contact equations. That is the whole job of Chapter 3.

What you already can do (Ch. 2)

  • Assign vectors to links and write a vector loop equation.
  • Resolve it into two scalar (x, y) position equations.
  • Solve in closed form or with Newton’s method.
  • Track points of interest on the coupler.

What you’ll add here (Ch. 3)

  • Write the rolling-contact equation for two bodies that roll without slipping.
  • Handle external, internal, and flat-surface contacts.
  • Account for the assembly configuration (initial values).
  • Analyze simple and planetary geartrains and compute gear, torque, and speed ratios.

Student objectives

By the end of this chapter you should be able to:

  • Investigate mechanisms that contain rolling contacts (rolling joints).
  • Derive and apply the rolling-contact equations so they can be incorporated into the vector loop method.
  • Distinguish externally contacting, internally contacting, and flat-surface rolling, and write the correct equation for each.
  • Identify the assembly configuration of a mechanism and include its initial-value terms in a position analysis.
  • Define a geartrain, classify it as simple or planetary, and compute its gear ratio R, torque ratio nt, and speed ratio ns.

Section 0

Foundations — the geometry you need first

Rolling contact is one of those ideas that feels obvious until you try to write it as an equation. Three small pieces of background make the rest of the chapter painless: arc length, rolling without slipping, and the difference between an absolute and a relative rotation.

Arc length: the bridge between turning and travelling

A point on the rim of a wheel of radius ρ that turns through an angle Δθ (in radians) sweeps out an arc of length

s = ρ · Δθ arc length

This single relationship is the seed of every rolling-contact equation. If two bodies roll on each other without slipping, the arc that rolls past the contact point on body x must equal the arc that rolls past on body y — the surfaces lay down the same length of “tape” against each other.

Think of it like… two tank treads pressed together

Picture two tanks parked nose-to-nose with their treads touching. If neither tread slips against the other, then every inch of tread that the left tank lays down is matched by exactly one inch on the right. The treads are “arc length.” A big-wheeled tank turns its wheel a little to lay down a foot of tread; a small-wheeled tank must spin its wheel a lot to lay down that same foot. That is precisely why a small gear spins faster than the large gear it meshes with: equal arc length, unequal radii, so unequal rotation.

Rolling without slipping

“Rolling without slipping” means the two surfaces have zero relative velocity at the point of contact. There is no skidding. This is the idealization the whole chapter rests on, and it is an excellent approximation for gears, friction wheels, and belts under normal load.

An important subtlety about gear teeth

Real gear teeth are not in pure rolling contact everywhere. The teeth actually have rolling contact only at the pitch point; at every other instant of the mesh there is some sliding. The genius of the standard involute tooth profile is that, despite this sliding, the net effect is exactly equivalent to two smooth imaginary circles — the pitch circles — rolling on each other without slipping. So in this chapter we model gears as their pitch circles rolling, and the equations come out clean.

Absolute vs. relative rotation — the notation that trips people up

The rolling-contact equations are written in terms of changes in vector direction, and they distinguish carefully between rotations measured against ground and rotations measured against another moving link. Get comfortable with this notation now:

Notation used throughout Chapter 3
SymbolMeaning
ρxRadius of rolling body x (its pitch radius, for a gear).
θxCurrent (absolute) direction angle of the vector attached to body x.
θxiInitial direction angle of that vector — the assembly configuration.
ΔθxAbsolute change in direction of body x:  θx − θxi.
Δθx/zRelative rotation of body x with respect to body z (the arm):  Δθx − Δθz.

The body labeled z is almost always the arm — the link that connects the centers of the two rolling bodies. Watch for it in every equation below.

Section 3.0

Rolling contacts — the family of rolling joints

A rolling contact — also called a rolling joint — is any place where two bodies touch and roll on one another. They are everywhere in machines, and they come in a surprisingly tidy table organized by two questions: are the centers far apart or close, and is the drive positive (teeth that cannot slip) or friction (surfaces that grip)?

The four kinds of rolling joints (after the course text)
Spacing of centersPositive driveFriction drive
WideChains & sprocketsBelts & pulleys
CloseGearsRolling wheels
Positive vs. friction drive, in one image

A positive drive is a zipper: the teeth interlock, so one side cannot slip past the other. A friction drive is two rubber erasers pressed together and rolled: they grip and turn together — until you push too hard, and they skid. Gears and chains are zippers; belts and friction wheels are erasers.

In the real world… your bicycle has both

A bicycle chain on its sprockets is a wide-spacing, positive rolling joint — the teeth guarantee no slip, so one pedal stroke always advances the wheel a fixed amount. The rubber tire on the road is a friction rolling joint — great until it rains and the wheel slips. Same chapter, two joints, on one machine.

Why the vector loop method needs help here

Recall the blind spot from the roadmap. When the vector loop method looks at two meshed gears, it treats them as two circular shapes whose centers are separated by ρx + ρy. The loop closes fine geometrically, but the loop equation never says that turning gear x forces gear y to turn in a fixed ratio. We must add that physics by hand. The statement we add is the rolling-contact equation.

The arm: why we always draw a link between the centers

Strictly speaking, two rolling bodies do not need a physical link joining their centers — the contact alone constrains them. But in practice something must hold the centers a fixed distance apart so the bodies stay in contact. That something is the arm. We label it body z, and it appears in every rolling-contact equation because rotations are most naturally measured relative to the arm.

Key idea to carry forward

Every rolling-contact equation below relates the rotation of body x and body y as seen from the arm z. The arm is the reference frame that makes rolling look simple. When the arm is fixed (bolted to ground), Δθz = 0 and the equations collapse to their simplest form — that is the case of a simple geartrain.

Section 3.1

Externally contacting rolling bodies

Two circles touch on their outsides — the everyday case of two meshing spur gears, or two friction wheels pressed rim-to-rim. As one turns clockwise, the other is dragged counter-clockwise. That opposite-sign behavior is the signature of external contact, and it shows up as a minus sign in the equation.

Deriving the equation

Equal arc length (no slipping) gives the magnitudes, and the opposite turning gives the sign. Measuring rotations relative to the arm z, the rolling-contact equation for external contact is:

ρx Δθx/z = −ρy Δθy/z (3.1)

Here Δθx/z is the rotation of body x relative to the arm, and likewise for y. The minus sign encodes “they spin in opposite directions as seen from the arm.” Expanding the relative rotations into absolute rotations and their initial values gives the full, computation-ready form:

ρx[(θx − θxi) − (θz − θzi)] = −ρy[(θy − θyi) − (θz − θzi)] (3.4)

Read it as: “(radius)×(how far this body turned past its start, minus how far the arm turned past its start).” The terms with subscript i are the assembly configuration (§3.4) — the initial directions of the vectors. Once those initial values are known, Equation (3.4) is just another scalar equation to add to your vector loop system.

Think of it like… two gears as opposite-spinning dance partners

Stand two people face to face and have them each spin in place. For their right hands to stay touching as they turn, one must spin clockwise while the other spins counter-clockwise — if they both spun the same way, their hands would tear apart. External gears are exactly these face-to-face partners: contact forces them to turn opposite ways, which is the minus sign in Equation (3.1).

In the real world… the direction reversal is a feature, not a bug

External meshing always reverses direction, which is why a single pair of gears makes the output spin “backwards.” Engineers exploit this constantly: the idler gear in a manual transmission’s reverse position is an extra external gear inserted purely to flip the output direction so the car can back up. And if you ever need the output to spin the same way as the input through a close-spaced pair, you reach for an internal gear instead — which is exactly §3.2.

Section 3.2

Internally contacting rolling bodies

Now one circle rolls inside the other — think of a small gear running around the inside of a large ring (an internal or annular gear). Here the surprising thing happens: the two bodies turn in the same direction as seen from the arm. The minus sign of external contact becomes a plus.

ρx(Δθx − Δθz) = ρy(Δθy − Δθz)
ρx[(θx − θxi) − (θz − θzi)] = ρy[(θy − θyi) − (θz − θzi)] (3.6)

Compare Equation (3.6) with Equation (3.4): the structure is identical, but the right-hand side is now positive. That single sign change is the entire difference between internal and external contact. Everything else — equal arc length, measuring relative to the arm, the initial-value terms — carries over unchanged.

Think of it like… a coin rolling inside a ring

Drop a quarter inside a large jar lid and roll it around the inner wall. Watch a mark on the quarter: as the quarter travels counter-clockwise around the inside, the mark also rotates counter-clockwise. Same direction. Contrast that with rolling the quarter around the outside of a second coin, where the mark would spin the opposite way. Inside ⇒ same sense (plus sign); outside ⇒ opposite sense (minus sign).

In the real world… the ring gear of every automatic transmission

The big internally-toothed ring (the annulus) in a planetary gearset is an internal contact: the planet gears roll on its inside surface and turn the same way it does. That same-direction property is what lets a planetary set achieve large reductions in a compact, coaxial package — the topic of §3.7. Internal contact also shows up in cycloidal drives and in the “hypocycloid” engines used in some compressors.

Section 3.3

One body with a flat surface

Let the radius of one body grow without bound and its surface flattens into a straight line. A circle rolling on a flat surface is the limiting case of external (or internal) contact — a wheel on a road, a gear on a rack. Now rolling converts rotation into straight-line travel, so the equation relates a change in length to a rotation.

ry − ryi = ρx(Δθx − Δθz)
ry − ryi = ρx[(θx − θxi) − (θz − θzi)] (3.8)

The left side, ry − ryi, is how far the contact point has travelled along the flat body from its start — a length, not an angle. The right side is the familiar arc length ρx·(relative rotation). This is just s = ρ·Δθ again, now with the “arc” unrolled onto a straight line.

Think of it like… unrolling a roll of tape onto the floor

Take a roll of tape and roll it along the floor without letting it skid. The length of tape laid on the floor equals the circumference that has passed under the roll: turn the roll one full revolution (2π radians) and you lay down exactly one circumference (2πρ) of tape. A rack-and-pinion is this exact picture frozen into steel: the round pinion is the roll of tape, the toothed rack is the floor, and Equation (3.8) says “distance the rack moved = arc the pinion rolled.”

In the real world… the steering in your car

Most cars steer with a rack-and-pinion: the steering column turns a small pinion, which rolls along a toothed rack, sliding it left or right to swing the front wheels. Equation (3.8) is literally the design equation — it tells the engineer how many turns of the wheel produce how many millimetres of rack travel, which sets how “quick” or “slow” the steering feels. The same mechanism raises and lowers the platform on a scissor lift and drives the gate on many sliding driveway gates.

Section 3.4

Assembly configuration — where the mechanism starts

Look back at Equations (3.4), (3.6), and (3.8). Each is written in terms of changes — how far each body has turned past its initial direction θxi, θyi, θzi. Those initial values are not arbitrary algebra: together they describe how the mechanism was assembled. We call them the assembly configuration.

Assembly configuration: the set of initial-value terms (the θi’s and any initial lengths) that appear when the rolling-contact equations are expanded. They must be known before a position analysis can be performed, because they fix the starting point from which all the Δθ’s are measured.

Why it matters: same motion, different result

This is the subtle part. Suppose you apply exactly the same set of changes — the same input rotation, the same arm motion — to two copies of a mechanism that were assembled differently. Because the changes are measured from different starting directions, the two copies end in different final positions. The motion is identical; the outcome is not. The assembly configuration is what breaks the tie.

Think of it like… two clocks set to different times

Take two identical clocks and advance both by exactly three hours. If one started at 12:00 and the other at 4:00, they now read 3:00 and 7:00 — same change applied, different readings, purely because they started differently. The clock’s starting time is its assembly configuration. In a mechanism, the “starting time” is the set of initial vector directions θi, and forgetting to specify them is like trying to read a clock that has no “12” marked.

Practical takeaway

When you set up a position analysis, the radii (ρ), link lengths, and the assembly-configuration angles (θi) are all known inputs — they go in the “scalar knowns” column. The joint variables you solve for are the “scalar unknowns.” You will see this knowns/unknowns bookkeeping made explicit in the worked examples.

Section 3.5

Geartrains

A geartrain is a mechanism built entirely of rolling contacts and the arms connecting their centers — nothing else. Because every joint is a rolling contact, every position equation of a geartrain is a rolling-contact equation. We stop writing vector loop equations for geartrains altogether; as you’ll see in Example 3.3, the loop equation degenerates to something meaningless like r̄1 − r̄1 = 0̄. The rolling-contact equations carry all the information.

Geartrain: a mechanism consisting entirely of rolling contacts and the arms connecting their centers. Its only position equations are rolling-contact equations.

Why bother? The one thing geartrains do better than anything else

A conventional linkage can transmit large torque, but the ratio between input and output speed varies as it moves. A geartrain’s defining virtue is a constant speed and torque ratio — turn the input at a steady rate and the output turns at a steady, perfectly proportional rate, forever. That constancy is the whole reason geartrains exist. The price is that a gear pair transmits less torque than a beefy linkage of the same size, which is why heavy-duty machines often combine the two.

In the real world… why your blender and your car both need this

You want a blender blade to spin at a steady high speed — a varying ratio would make the blade surge and stall. You want a car’s wheels to turn at a fixed proportion of engine speed in each gear so the speedometer means something. Constant ratio is non-negotiable in both, so both use geartrains rather than linkages for the speed conversion.

Geartrain terminology

Vocabulary for classifying geartrains
TermDefinition

Planetary geartrainContains rolling elements whose arms are not fixed — the arm itself rotates, carrying gear centers in orbit. (Also called epicyclic.)
Think of it like… a merry-go-round vs. a record player

In a simple geartrain the gear centers are pinned down — like records spinning on fixed turntables that happen to touch. In a planetary geartrain the arm spins, so the gear centers themselves ride around like horses on a merry-go-round while also spinning on their own posts. That double motion (orbit + spin) is what makes planetary trains powerful and what makes their equations richer — the arm term Δθz no longer vanishes.

Section 3.6

Gear, torque, and speed ratios

A geartrain is bought and sold on its ratios. Three are defined, and — pleasingly — they all turn out to be the same number dressed up three ways.

The three geartrain ratios
RatioSymbolDefinition
Gear ratioRNumber of rotations of the input that cause one rotation of the output.
Torque rationtTorque increase (or decrease) created by the transmission.
Speed rationsSpeed decrease (or increase) created by the transmission.

The ratio equations

R = Δθin / Δθout (3.18)
nt = Tout / Tin  →  Tout = nt Tin (3.19)
nt = −R (3.21)
ns = ωout / ωin  →  ωout = ns ωin (3.22)
ns = 1 / R (3.24)

Collecting them gives the single most useful line in the chapter:

R = −nt = 1 / ns

In words: a train that makes the input turn R times per output turn also multiplies torque by R (up to sign) and divides speed by R. You cannot get something for nothing — trade speed for torque or torque for speed, in exact proportion. (This is conservation of power, P = Tω, in disguise.)

Think of it like… a low gear on a bicycle climbing a hill

Shift to a low gear and you pedal many times for each slow turn of the wheel (large R). In exchange the wheel pushes much harder against the hill (torque up by R) even though it turns slowly (speed down by R). Shift to a high gear for flat ground and the trade reverses: one pedal stroke spins the wheel fast but with little force. The gear ratio is the exchange rate between “how fast” and “how hard.”

From tooth counts to ratios — the practical step

The rolling-contact equation gives ratios in terms of pitch-circle radii ρ — but radii are rarely marked on a real gear. What is always knowable is the number of teeth N. Because two meshing gears must have the same tooth size (same spacing along the pitch circle), their radii are proportional to their tooth counts:

2πρx/Nx = 2πρy/Ny  →  ρx/ρy = Nx/Ny

So wherever a derivation gives you a ratio of radii, you may swap in the ratio of tooth counts. For the single-stage simple train of Example 3.3, R = −ρ3/ρ2 = −N3/N2.

In the real world… reading a gearbox spec sheet

When a catalog lists a planetary gearhead as “100:1,” that is the gear ratio R. It tells you the motor turns 100 times for each output turn, the output torque is about 100× the motor torque (minus friction losses), and the output speed is 1/100 of the motor speed. A “4.10:1 final drive” in a car axle means the driveshaft turns 4.10 times per wheel revolution — multiplying engine torque to the wheels by 4.10. You can now derive every one of those numbers from tooth counts.

Section 3.7

Planetary (epicyclic) geartrains

In a planetary geartrain the arm is no longer fixed — it rotates, carrying one or more planet gears in orbit around a central sun gear, while the planets also mesh with an outer internally-toothed ring (annulus). Three coaxial members — sun, carrier (arm), ring — share one axis. This is the geartrain you cannot build from fixed centers, and it is the most important geartrain in modern machinery.

The three members

  • Sun — the central gear on the main axis.
  • Planet(s) — gears that mesh with the sun (external contact) and the ring (internal contact), while orbiting.
  • Ring / annulus — the outer internally-toothed gear.
  • Carrier (arm) — the link that holds the planet centers and rotates with their orbit. This is body z.

Why the arm term matters now

For a simple train we set Δθz = 0 and the rolling-contact equations simplified. In a planetary train the arm moves, so Δθz ≠ 0 and you must keep every arm term in Equations (3.4) and (3.6). The payoff is remarkable flexibility: by choosing which of the three members you hold fixed, the same hardware delivers three completely different ratios.

Think of it like… the Moon orbiting the Earth

The Moon orbits the Earth (that orbit is the arm’s rotation) while also rotating on its own axis. Because the two motions are locked together, we always see the same face of the Moon. A planet gear does the same dance: it spins on its post and rides around the sun, and the rolling-contact equations are simply the bookkeeping that keeps the “spin” and the “orbit” consistent. This is exactly why planetary trains are also called epicyclic — “epi-cyclic” means a circle riding on a circle.

In the real world… the Prius, your drill, and every automatic transmission

Planetary gearsets are everywhere precisely because of the “hold one member, get a new ratio” trick. An automatic transmission engages brakes and clutches to hold different members, producing each forward gear from one compact gearset. A cordless drill stacks two or three planetary stages to turn a fast, weak motor into a slow, strong chuck. And the Toyota Prius hybrid uses a single planetary set as a “power-split device,” blending engine and electric-motor inputs into one output — exploiting the planetary’s ability to combine two inputs into one. A wind-turbine gearbox runs the trick in reverse, stepping a slow, powerful rotor up to generator speed.

Going Deeper

Gear-tooth geometry — why the equations work

Everything above modeled gears as smooth pitch circles rolling without slipping. Real gears have teeth, and teeth obviously do not roll smoothly — they push on each other. This section answers the question that should be nagging you: how can toothed gears behave exactly like rolling pitch circles? The answer is a beautiful piece of geometry called the involute, and understanding it tells you how real gears are specified, meshed, and kept from destroying themselves.

The fundamental law of gearing

For two gears to deliver a perfectly constant speed ratio (the whole reason geartrains exist), their tooth profiles must satisfy one rule:

Fundamental law of gearing: the common normal to the two tooth surfaces at their point of contact must pass through a fixed point on the line of centers, called the pitch point, in every position of the mesh.

If that holds, the two profiles are called conjugate and the angular-velocity ratio stays constant throughout the mesh. Many curves can be conjugate, but one is so convenient that it dominates all modern gearing: the involute of a circle.

Rolling at one point, sliding everywhere else

Here is the subtlety from §0 made precise: meshing teeth are in pure rolling only at the pitch point. At every other instant of the engagement there is some sliding between the surfaces. The involute’s genius is that despite this sliding, the net kinematic effect is identical to two pitch circles rolling without slip — so all of §3.1–3.7 holds exactly. (This is also why gears need lubrication: that sliding generates friction and heat.)

The involute, built from a string

Take a circle — the base circle of radius rb — and wrap a taut string around it. Hold a pencil at the end of the string and unwind it, keeping it tight. The curve the pencil traces is the involute. That is the working face of a standard gear tooth.

Think of it like… unspooling a yo-yo against a wall

Imagine the string of a yo-yo wrapped around its axle, and you unspool it while keeping it taut. The tip of the string sweeps a curve that is always perpendicular to the string — and the string is always tangent to the axle. That “always tangent to the base circle, always perpendicular to the path” property is exactly what makes the involute satisfy the law of gearing: the line of contact (the taut string) keeps a fixed direction, so the contact normal always passes through the same pitch point.

Pressure angle — the tilt of the push

Because contact happens along that taut-string direction (the line of action), the force one tooth exerts on another is not aimed straight along the motion — it is tilted. The angle between the line of action and the common tangent to the pitch circles is the pressure angle φ. It connects the base and pitch circles by a wonderfully simple relation:

rb = r · cosφ base circle

Standard gears use φ = 20° (older systems used 14.5°; some heavy-duty gears use 25°). A larger pressure angle makes teeth stubbier and stronger but increases the separating force that tries to push the gears apart.

Tooth size: module and diametral pitch

Two gears can only mesh if their teeth are the same size. Tooth size is captured by one number, expressed two ways:

Module m (metric)

m = d / N mm per tooth

Pitch diameter d divided by tooth count N. A module-2 gear with 40 teeth has a pitch diameter of 80 mm. Two gears mesh only if they share the same module.

Diametral pitch P (US)

P = N / d teeth per inch

Tooth count per inch of pitch diameter — the inverse idea. Note m and P are reciprocals (with a unit conversion): big module = coarse, big teeth; big diametral pitch = fine, small teeth.

Because d = m·N, the pitch radius is ρ = r = m·N/2. Substitute that into any ratio from §3.6 and the module cancels — which is exactly why tooth counts alone determine the gear ratio, R = −N3/N2. The module sets the size of the gears; the tooth-count ratio sets the ratio.

In the real world… reading a gear catalog

A gear listed as “module 1.5, 20° PA, 24T” tells you everything: 1.5 mm of pitch diameter per tooth (so d = 36 mm), a 20° pressure angle, 24 teeth. To mesh with it you need another module-1.5, 20° gear — any tooth count you like, and the count you pick sets the ratio. Mixing modules or pressure angles is the classic beginner mistake that leaves you with two gears that simply will not run together.

Interference and undercutting

The involute profile is only defined outside the base circle. If a small gear with few teeth meshes with a much larger one, the tip of the big gear’s tooth can try to contact the little gear below its base circle, where no proper involute exists. The result is interference: the tip gouges the root of the mating tooth. When gears are cut, this shows up as undercutting — the cutter removes material at the tooth root, weakening it.

Three standard cures for interference

  • Use more teeth on the pinion. With a 20° pressure angle, a pinion of about 17 or more teeth avoids interference against a rack — a rule of thumb worth memorizing.
  • Increase the pressure angle (e.g. 20° → 25°), which shrinks the non-involute region near the base circle.
  • Profile shift (addendum modification): cut the teeth slightly farther out so contact stays above the base circle.
Think of it like… a key cut too deep

Interference is like a key whose teeth are cut so deep they bite into the body of the lock rather than just turning the pins. The fix is the same in spirit: don’t cut where the working surface isn’t supposed to reach. A gear avoids “cutting too deep” by having enough teeth, a steeper pressure angle, or a shifted profile so all contact stays on the valid involute region above the base circle.

How this connects back to the chapter

None of this geometry changes the rolling-contact equations — that is the whole point of the involute. The pitch circles still roll without slipping, so ρxΔθx/z = ∓ρyΔθy/z and R = −N3/N2 remain exactly true. Gear-tooth geometry is what lets a manufacturable, force-transmitting part behave like the idealized rolling circle your equations assume.

Worked examples

Example 3.1 — A five-bar mechanism with a rolling contact

Problem. A five-bar mechanism has one of its coupler links, body 4, in rolling contact with the ground (body 1). Link 5 is the “arm” connecting the rolling centers. Derive the mechanism’s position equations and deduce the number of degrees of freedom.

Figure E3.1 — The five-bar mechanism with body 4 (the wheel) rolling on body 1 (the ground). Link 5 is the practical arm keeping the wheel down. The five vectors form the closed loop r̄2 + r̄3 − r̄4 − r̄5 + r̄1 = 0̄.

A puzzle about mobility

Run Grübler’s criterion on the full five links (with the arm) and you get F = 0 — it looks locked. The arm, link 5, is the culprit: it is not kinematically necessary, only practically necessary to keep body 4 touching the ground. Remove link 5 from the count and Grübler gives the correct F = 1. This is a classic case where a redundant practical link fools the mobility formula.

Show the full solution

Step 1 — Vector loop. A suitable loop gives r̄2 + r̄3 − r̄4 − r̄5 + r̄1 = 0̄. With θ1 = 0 the two scalar component equations are:

r2cosθ2 + r3cosθ3 − r4cosθ4 − r5cosθ5 + r1 = 0 (3.11)
r2sinθ2 + r3sinθ3 − r4sinθ4 − r5sinθ5 = 0 (3.12)

Step 2 — Rolling-contact equation. Body 4 rolls externally on body 1, with arm body 5. Using Equation (3.1) with x=4, y=1, z=5:

ρ4Δθ4/5 = −ρ1Δθ1/5

that is, ρ4(Δθ4 − Δθ5) = −ρ1(Δθ1 − Δθ5). Since body 1 is fixed, Δθ1 = 0, so:

ρ4(Δθ4 − Δθ5) = ρ1Δθ5

Step 3 — Incorporate the assembly configuration and write in homogeneous form:

ρ4[(θ4 − θ4i) − (θ5 − θ5i)] = ρ1(θ5 − θ5i)
ρ4(θ4 − θ4i) − (ρ1 + ρ4)(θ5 − θ5i) = 0 (3.13)

Step 4 — Count. Equations (3.11), (3.12), (3.13) are three position equations.

scalar knowns  r1, θ1=0, r2, r3, r4, r5, ρ1, ρ4, θ4i, θ5i

scalar unknowns  θ2, θ3, θ4, θ5

Result. Three equations in four unknowns ⇒ one of the unknowns must be supplied as the input. The mechanism has one degree of freedom — matching the corrected Grübler result. Notice that the rolling radii (ρ1, ρ4) and the assembly-configuration angles (θ4i, θ5i) are knowns, exactly as §3.4 promised.

Example 3.3 — Gear ratio of a single-stage simple geartrain

Problem. In the single-stage simple geartrain (the two gears of §3.5, with gear 2 as input and gear 3 as output), derive the position equation, deduce the degrees of freedom, and compute the gear ratio R.

Figure E3.3 — The single-stage simple geartrain. Body 1 (the ground / arm) holds both centers fixed at a distance r1 = ρ2 + ρ3. With body 1 fixed, Δθ1 = 0 and the rolling-contact equation alone gives R = −ρ3/ρ2 = −N3/N2.

Show the full solution

Step 1 — The vector loop is meaningless. The only loop vector r̄1 runs between the two fixed centers and back, giving r̄1 − r̄1 = 0̄ — no information. As promised in §3.5, the only meaningful equations for a geartrain are rolling-contact equations.

Step 2 — Rolling-contact equation. The contact is external (gear 3 = x, gear 2 = y, ground = arm = body 1 = z):

ρ3Δθ3/1 = −ρ2Δθ2/1
ρ3(Δθ3 − Δθ1) = −ρ2(Δθ2 − Δθ1)

Body 1 (the arm) is fixed, so Δθ1 = 0 (this is what makes it a simple train):

ρ3Δθ3 = −ρ2Δθ2  →  ρ3Δθ3 + ρ2Δθ2 = 0 (3.25)

Step 3 — Assembly configuration:

ρ3(θ3 − θ3i) + ρ2(θ2 − θ2i) = 0

scalar knowns  ρ3, ρ2, θ2i, θ3i  ·  scalar unknowns  θ2, θ3

One equation, two unknowns ⇒ one degree of freedom. Supply the input θ2 and the output θ3 follows.

Step 4 — Gear ratio. From Equation (3.25), with R = Δθin/Δθout = Δθ2/Δθ3:

R = Δθ2/Δθ3 = −ρ3/ρ2 (3.26)

Because R < 0, Δθ2 and Δθ3 are in opposite directions (external mesh). The torque ratio nt = −R = ρ3/ρ2 > 0, so the driving torque T2 and the load torque T3 act in the same sense.

Step 5 — Convert to tooth counts. Since meshing gears share tooth size, ρ3/ρ2 = N3/N2, so the practical answer is:

R = −N3/N2

You rarely know the pitch radii of a real gear, but you can always count teeth — so this is the form an engineer actually uses.

The thread connecting both examples

Both examples follow the identical recipe: write whatever vector loop equations exist, add a rolling-contact equation for each rolling joint, fold in the assembly-configuration initial values, then count knowns against unknowns to find the degrees of freedom. The geartrain example is just the limiting case where the loop equations vanish and the rolling-contact equation does all the work.

Definitions

  • Rolling joint two bodies that roll without slipping.
  • Arm (z) link joining the rolling centers; the reference for Δθ.
  • Assembly config the initial values θi that fix the start.
  • Stage = number of rolling contacts.
  • Simple train arm fixed (Δθz=0).
  • Planetary arm rotates (Δθz≠0).

Sign & sense quick rule

  • External mesh opposite directions, R < 0.
  • Internal mesh same direction, R > 0.
  • Flat / rack rotation ↔ straight travel.
  • Planetary fit Nring = Nsun + 2Nplanet.
  • Fixed-ring ωcarrier/ωsun = Nsun/(Nsun+Nring).
