Welcome

From analysis to design

For seven chapters we've taken mechanisms as given and asked what they do — positions, velocities, accelerations, forces, energies. This chapter flips the question. We start with a desired behavior — "input angle φ should produce output angle ψ according to this function" — and we work backward to find the link lengths that deliver it. That's synthesis, and the cleanest analytical handle on it is Freudenstein's equation.

Mechanism synthesis. The design of a mechanism (its dimensions, geometry, and configuration) to produce a specified motion. Three classical flavors: function generation (input-output relationship), path generation (a point traces a curve), and motion generation (a body passes through prescribed poses). This chapter is about function generation.

What you'll be able to do after this module

  • State and use Freudenstein's equation (Eq. 8.5) for the four-bar mechanism.
  • Design a four-bar by point-matching: pick three precision points, solve a 3×3 linear system, get link lengths.
  • Design a four-bar by derivative-matching: pick one design position, match a value and its first three derivatives via the kinematic-coefficient ladder.
  • Recognize when one method is the right tool versus the other.
  • Recreate Stanišić's Example 8.1 (bridge-lifter) and Example 8.2 (rolling-gear) end-to-end.
Think of it like…

Synthesis is the difference between reading a recipe and writing one. Analysis is "I have this linkage — what does it do?" Synthesis is "I want this behavior — what linkage do I build?" Freudenstein's equation is the kitchen scale that lets you write the recipe in numbers instead of guesses.

In the real world

The function generators in this chapter live in places you don't think to look: an automotive windshield-wiper linkage that wipes wide and pauses at each end, a bascule bridge that lifts a span to clear a sailboat's mast, an aircraft landing-gear retraction mechanism that folds up exactly into its bay, and a prosthetic knee whose four-bar geometry mimics a natural gait. Every one of those started as someone solving Freudenstein's equation for three precision points.

Foundations

What you need before we start

This chapter sits on top of the four-bar vector loop you learned in Chapter 2 and the angular convention from Chapter 4. A quick refresh, then we're synthesizing.

The four-bar vector loop, one more time

Four links, four joints. Link 1 is ground (length r1, lying along the +X axis from O2 to O4). Link 2 is the input lever, rotating about O2. Link 3 is the coupler. Link 4 is the output lever, rotating about O4. The closure condition is the loop equation:

(8.1) r⃗2 + r⃗3 − r⃗4 − r⃗1 = 0⃗

What the angles mean

  • θ2 — orientation of link 2 (input lever), measured from +X to r2.
  • θ4 — orientation of link 4 (output lever), measured from +X to r4.
  • φ — input rotation: how far link 2 has turned from some chosen initial position θ2i. So φ = θ2 − θ2i.
  • ψ — output rotation: how far link 4 has turned from its initial position θ4i. So ψ = θ4 − θ4i.
Watch the bookkeeping

θ2 and θ4 are positions — absolute angles in the global frame. φ and ψ are rotations — changes from an arbitrary starting pose you get to pick. The function you want to generate, f(φ,ψ) = 0, lives in the φ-ψ space. Freudenstein's equation lives in the θ2-θ4 space. Most of the work in synthesis is shuttling between the two.

Think of it like…

θ2 and θ4 are compass bearings; φ and ψ are turn signals. The compass tells you where you're pointing; the turn signal tells you how much you've changed direction from where you started. The relationship "turn left twice as far as you steer right" is a statement about turn signals — once you also know which way the car started, you can convert it to compass bearings.

The three flavors of synthesis

Function generation

Input rotation φ and output rotation ψ are tied by some function f(φ,ψ) = 0. This is Chapter 8.

Path generation

A specific point (usually on the coupler) is required to trace a specific curve.

Motion generation

An entire body (with both position and orientation) must pass through a sequence of poses.

In the real world

Function generation shows up wherever an input rotation must compute an output rotation: a steering linkage (Ackermann) where inner and outer wheels must turn by related-but-different angles, a film-projector intermittent feed, a watch's chronograph reset. Path generation is famously the Hoekens straight-line linkage. Motion generation is what positions an excavator bucket to dump its load at a specific angle. Different flavor, same Freudenstein-style algebra.

Beyond Stanišić · A bit of history

Ferdinand Freudenstein (1926–2006), an MIT-trained mechanical engineer at Columbia, wrote the 1955 paper that bears his name. Before Freudenstein, kinematic synthesis was largely a graphical art — compasses, French curves, and a great deal of patience. The equation we're about to derive lets you replace all of that with a 3×3 linear solve. Roth (2007) calls him "the father of modern kinematic synthesis." His machinery still underlies essentially every commercial linkage-design tool sold today.

§ 8.1 · The Key Equation

Freudenstein's equation for the four-bar

The vector loop in Eq. 8.1, written in components and rearranged to eliminate the coupler angle θ3, gives the cleanest possible algebraic statement of the four-bar input-output relationship. Here is the derivation in five steps, then the result you'll spend the rest of the chapter using.

Show the derivation (5 steps)

Step 1. Write Eq. 8.1 in scalar form by taking the X and Y components.

r2 cosθ2 + r3 cosθ3 − r4 cosθ4 − r1 = 0
r2 sinθ2 + r3 sinθ3 − r4 sinθ4 = 0

Step 2. Isolate the coupler terms.

r3 cosθ3 = r1 + r4 cosθ4 − r2 cosθ2
r3 sinθ3 = r4 sinθ4 − r2 sinθ2

Step 3. Square both and add. The coupler angle θ3 vanishes via cos² + sin² = 1.

r3² = (r1 + r4 cosθ4 − r2 cosθ2)² + (r4 sinθ4 − r2 sinθ2)²

Step 4. Expand and collect. After grouping like terms (and using the cos(A−B) identity on the cross-product), you land at:

r1² + r2² − r3² + r4² + 2r1r4 cosθ4 − 2r1r2 cosθ2 − 2r2r4 cos(θ2 − θ4) = 0

Step 5. Divide through by 2r2r4 and group the link-length terms into three dimensionless ratios. That gives the final form.

The equation itself

(8.5) · Freudenstein R3 + R2 cosθ4 − R1 cosθ2 = cos(θ2 − θ4)

Where the three Freudenstein ratios are dimensionless combinations of the four link lengths:

(8.4) R1 = r1 / r4    R2 = r1 / r2    R3 = (r2² − r3² + r4² + r1²) / (2 r2 r4)
Why this is a big deal

Eq. 8.5 has three unknowns (the Rs) and is linear in them. That means three input-output pairs — three precision points — give you exactly three equations in three unknowns. A 3×3 linear solve is high-school algebra. Compare with trying to solve the original loop equation for four link lengths directly: that's a nonlinear nightmare. Freudenstein's gift is the change of variables.

Think of it like…

The Freudenstein ratios R1, R2, R3 are like the Cartesian coordinates of a recipe. The four link lengths are the ingredients; the ratios are proportions. Two recipes with the same proportions taste the same — and two four-bars with the same Freudenstein ratios produce the same input-output curve. That "scale doesn't matter" property is exactly what we'll lose when we get to the crank-slider in Part II.

§ 8.1.1 · Method 1

Point-matching: hit three targets exactly

Suppose you want the linkage to satisfy three specific input-output pairs — three pairs of (φ, ψ) that the customer hands you. These are called precision points. Point-matching finds a four-bar that hits all three exactly. Between them, the actual curve drifts away from the desired curve — but at the three points themselves, you're spot on.

Think of it like…

Point-matching is Lagrange interpolation for mechanisms. Give a polynomial three (x, y) points and it picks the unique quadratic that hits them; give Freudenstein three (θ2, θ4) points and it picks the unique R1, R2, R3 that hit them. The fit is exact at the sample points, and you live with whatever happens between them.

The setup

You're given three (φk, ψk) pairs for k = 1, 2, 3. To use Eq. 8.5, you need to translate them into (θ2, θ4) pairs. That requires two free choices on your part: the starting angles θ2i and θ4i. With those picked, Eqs. 8.6 and 8.7 do the conversion:

(8.6, 8.7) θ2,k = φk + θ2i     θ4,k = ψk + θ4i

The system

Now write Eq. 8.5 three times — once for each precision point — and collect into matrix form:

(8.9) 
[ −cosθ2,1   cosθ4,1   1 ] [R1]   [ cos(θ2,1 − θ4,1) ]
[ −cosθ2,2   cosθ4,2   1 ] [R2] = [ cos(θ2,2 − θ4,2) ]
[ −cosθ2,3   cosθ4,3   1 ] [R3]   [ cos(θ2,3 − θ4,3) ]

The recipe

  1. Take the three desired (φk, ψk) precision points.
  2. Choose θ2i and θ4i (your free choices — these reshape the resulting design).
  3. Convert (φ, ψ) → (θ2, θ4) for each k.
  4. Build the 3×3 system (8.9) and solve for R1, R2, R3.
  5. Assume one link length (typically r1, the ground, since it's set by your application).
  6. Back out the other three from Eq. 8.4: r4 = r1/R1, r2 = r1/R2, and finally r3 from R3's definition.
Sign warning

If a back-solve for r2 or r4 gives a negative number, that doesn't mean failure. It means the vector for that link points opposite to the direction you assumed when you set θ2 or θ4. Flip the assumed direction and the design is still valid.

Beyond Stanišić · Chebyshev spacing of precision points

Where should the precision points go across the input range? Pafnuty Chebyshev (1854) showed that for any function being approximated by a polynomial, the points that minimize the worst-case error are not evenly spaced — they cluster slightly near the ends of the interval. For three precision points spanning φ ∈ [φmin, φmax], the Chebyshev locations are:

φk = φ̄ − (Δφ/2) cos((2k−1)π/6)    for k = 1, 2, 3

For φ ∈ [0°, 180°], that's approximately {12°, 90°, 168°} — versus Stanišić's evenly-spaced {45°, 90°, 135°}. In Example 8.1 the difference shrinks the maximum structural error by roughly half. Stanišić doesn't cover this; most other textbooks do, and any commercial synthesis tool uses it by default.

In the real world

A windshield-wiper four-bar is point-matched to three precision points: blade-fully-left, blade-vertical, blade-fully-right. Choose well and the wiper sweeps cleanly with a pause at each extreme; choose poorly and the blade flies off the windshield at the ends. The classical "Watt's linkage" used in 19th-century steam engines is itself a function-generator designed graphically before Freudenstein existed.

§ 8.1.2 · Method 2

Derivative-matching: hit one position perfectly

Point-matching nails three points and lets the curve drift between them. The alternative is to nail one point — and not just the position, but the slope, curvature, and rate-of-change-of-curvature at that point. That's derivative-matching: match a value of θ4 together with its first three derivatives with respect to θ2.

Think of it like…

Derivative-matching is Taylor series for mechanisms. A Taylor series matches the value, slope, curvature, and so on at a single point — and the result is highly accurate near that point, with error growing as you move away. Same trade here. Point-matching is interpolation (great at three spots, mediocre between); derivative-matching is local approximation (perfect at one spot, very good in its neighborhood).

The kinematic coefficients of synthesis

Define three derivatives of the output angle θ4 with respect to the input angle θ2:

(8.12) h4 = dθ4/dθ2     h′4 = d²θ4/dθ2²     h″4 = d³θ4/dθ2³

These are the kinematic coefficients you met in Chapter 4. They are purely geometric — they depend only on the link lengths and the current angles, not on time or speed. From Eq. 8.8 (φ = θ2 − θ2i, ψ = θ4 − θ4i), the φ-ψ derivatives and the θ2-θ4 derivatives are identical:

h4 = dψ/dφ     h′4 = d²ψ/dφ²     h″4 = d³ψ/dφ³

So if the customer says "I want ψ = f(φ)," differentiate that function three times, evaluate at the design position φdes, and you've got the three target values to match.

The reduced equation

Differentiating Freudenstein's equation three times, eliminating intermediate derivatives, and reorganizing the algebra (the textbook spends 14 equations on this) lands at a remarkably tidy result: a quadratic in tanθ2.

(8.26) λ1 tan²θ2 + λ2 tanθ2 + λ3 = 0
Pay attention to what just happened

Three derivative conditions, plus one position, plus one assumed link length is five conditions. We have five unknowns: R1, R2, R3, θ2i, θ4i (the last two come from your choice of θ2des). The system is square. The price of the squeeze is that we now need to compute a tower of intermediate quantities.

The supplementary tower

The three λ's are built out of nine "intermediate" quantities (D1, D2, D3, E1, E2, E3, F1, F2, F3), which themselves are built out of twelve constants (ai, bi, ci, di for i = 1, 2, 3) that depend on the three target kinematic coefficients.

Each row of Table 8.3 gives an (ai, bi, ci, di), and at the chosen design angle θ4des these combine into:

(8.18) Ei = ai sinθ4 + bi cosθ4    Di = ci cosθ4 + di sinθ4    Fi = −ci sinθ4 + di cosθ4

Eq. 8.27 — the lambdas

(8.27) λ1 = D2(E3 − E1) + E2(D1 − D3)
λ2 = E2(F1 − F3) − D1E3 + E1D3 + F2(E3 − E1)
λ3 = E1F3 − F1E3

Eqs. 8.20, 8.21, 8.22, 8.23 — finishing the ratios

Once you've solved Eq. 8.26 for tanθ2 and picked one of the two θ2des roots, R1 and R2 follow from a Cramer-style ratio, and R3 drops out of Freudenstein's equation evaluated at the design pair:

(8.20) R1 = G1 / G     R2 = G2 / G
(8.21) G = E2 sinθ2 − E1 cosθ2
(8.22) G1 = (D1sinθ2 + F1cosθ2)E2 − (D2sinθ2 + F2cosθ2)E1
(8.23) G2 = (D2sinθ2 + F2cosθ2)sinθ2 − (D1sinθ2 + F1cosθ2)cosθ2
Don't memorize this — manage it

Nobody, including the textbook author, recommends that you commit Eqs. 8.18 through 8.27 to memory. The whole point of the design procedure on the next section is to give you a checklist that walks through these in order. Treat the formulas like a spreadsheet: row by row, you'll know exactly which one to use next.

§ 8.1.3 · The Checklist

The 11-step derivative-matching procedure

Stanišić's design procedure organizes the whole derivative-matching computation into eleven steps. Walk through them in order, with a calculator (or, more pragmatically, MATLAB — see the MATLAB module), and you'll always land at four link lengths.

  1. Pick a design position. Choose (φdes, ψdes) satisfying the desired f(φ,ψ) = 0. This is the point where the three derivatives will be matched exactly.
  2. Compute the three kinematic-coefficient targets h4, h′4, h″4 by differentiating the desired function three times and evaluating at φdes.
  3. Fill in Table 8.3 using the three target h's.
  4. Assume a design value of θ4, called θ4des. This is a free choice. Then compute Di, Ei, Fi for i = 1, 2, 3 from Eq. 8.18.
  5. Compute λ1, λ2, λ3 from Eq. 8.27.
  6. Solve the quadratic Eq. 8.26 for tanθ2. You get two solutions.
  7. Pick a θ2 from each tan-root. Each tan-root gives two angles (180° apart) but they collapse to the same mechanism, so you get effectively two distinct designs. Choose one as θ2des.
  8. Compute G, G1, G2 from Eqs. 8.21–8.23.
  9. Compute R1, R2 from Eq. 8.20, and R3 from Eq. 8.5 evaluated at (θ2des, θ4des).
  10. Assume one link length (typically r1 set by your application). Back out the other three via Eq. 8.4.
  11. Compute the initial angles θ2i = θ2des − φdes and θ4i = θ4des − ψdes. Mechanism is now fully defined.

In the real world

Derivative-matching is the right tool when you care most about behavior near a specific operating point. A prosthetic knee's four-bar must reproduce natural knee kinematics most accurately at the stance phase — the position where the user's weight is on it. A landing-gear retraction linkage must lock up smoothly at the retracted position, so its second derivative (effectively the rate at which the locking force ramps up) is what matters there. In both cases, you pick that operating point and derivative-match it.

Example 8.1 — A bridge-lifter (point-matching)

The problem

Design a four-bar mechanism to replace a 2:1 gear pair, so that the output rotates exactly half as far as the input over the working range. This is the linkage that lifts a bascule bridge to clear a sailboat's mast: a slow input rotation through a large arc moves the bridge deck through a smaller arc, with the linkage providing the torque multiplication that gears would otherwise.

Desired function: ψ = φ/2, equivalently f(φ,ψ) = ψ − φ/2 = 0, over the range 0° ≤ φ ≤ 180° (so 0° ≤ ψ ≤ 90°).

Design takeaway

There are an infinity to the fifth power of valid choices here — three precision points (each free to slide along the desired curve) plus the two starting angles. The set of valid four-bars satisfying the same function generation problem is enormous. The skill of mechanism design is picking the choices that also give good transmission angles, reasonable link lengths, and a compact footprint. The bridge-lifter shown in Figure 8.6 of the textbook is one good choice out of infinitely many.

Example 8.2 — A rolling-gear function (derivative-matching)

Desired function: ψ = −φ + 10°, over the range −100° < φ < 10° (so 0° < ψ < 110°). This corresponds to a pair of externally rolling gears with equal pitch radii — R = −10.

r4 = 10 / 2.828 ≈ 3.54 in

Step 6 · Verify with Tool 3

The mechanism's local slope at the design position is exactly dψ/dφ = −1 (matching the linear target), and because both h′ and h″ are zero there, the curvature and the rate-of-change-of-curvature are also matched. The result is a near-perfect linear ψ(φ) over a remarkably wide range — that's the payoff of matching three derivatives, not just one. Open Tool 3 and click "Load Example 8.2" to see it live.

Why the rolling-gear analogy?

Two external gears with equal pitch radii produce exactly ψ = −φ (same magnitude, opposite direction). The +10° is just a phase offset that comes from picking which tooth is meshed at φ = 0. So this whole exercise is asking: can a four-bar mimic a pair of equal external gears? Yes — over a useful range, derivative-matching makes it indistinguishable. That's how you replace a gear pair with a linkage when noise, lubrication, or wear rule out gears (think medical devices and surgical robotics).

Wrap-Up

Objectives recap

Before moving to Part II (Crank-Slider), check that each of these is solid. If any one isn't, jump back to the linked section.

  • I can write down Freudenstein's equation from memory and explain what each ratio means. → § Freudenstein
  • I can take three (φ, ψ) precision points, pick θ2i and θ4i, build the 3×3 system, solve it, and extract four link lengths. → § Point-matching
  • I can compute h4, h′4, h″4 from a desired ψ(φ) and walk the 11-step derivative-matching procedure to a design. → § Procedure
  • I can explain when to use point-matching vs. derivative-matching, in terms of how the resulting curve behaves (Lagrange interpolation vs. Taylor approximation). → § Derivative-matching
  • I can reproduce Examples 8.1 and 8.2 from the textbook, end-to-end. → § Examples
  • I know that in 4-bar synthesis, scale is free — the same ratios produce the same input-output curve at any size. → § Tool 1
