Chapter Roadmap

Where this chapter fits

Through Chapters 2–4 you became fluent in kinematics: positions, velocities, and accelerations of a mechanism, with no mention of why it moves. Chapter 5 turns the corner into dynamics — the relationship between motion and the forces and torques that cause or accompany it. We start with the most common engineering question of all: “If I want this machine to move exactly like this, how big a motor do I need, and how hard are the bearings being pushed?”

That is the Inverse Dynamics Problem (IDP): the state of motion is known (you already solved it in earlier chapters), and you work backward to the forces and torques that must be present. It is the workhorse of machine design — sizing motors, selecting bearings, checking that links won’t bend or break. Everything in this chapter is Newton’s Laws applied carefully to one link at a time.

Think of it like…

…reading a detective story backward. In a forward dynamics problem you’re handed the forces and asked “what happens?” — you watch the crime unfold. In the inverse problem you already know exactly what happened (the full motion), and your job is to deduce who pushed whom, and how hard. The motion is the evidence; the forces are the culprits you reconstruct. Engineers love the inverse problem because the “evidence” (the motion) is usually what the customer specifies, and the “culprits” (motor torque, bearing loads) are exactly what they need to buy and build.

What you’ll be able to do

  • State the difference between the inverse and forward dynamics problems, and identify which one a real design task is asking.
  • Write the planar force and moment equations for a link — both about its mass center G and about an arbitrary point A.
  • Draw free body diagrams using the standard notation (squiggly, dashed, and solid vectors) that encodes what is known and unknown.
  • Explain why a “planar” machine is really partially three-dimensional, and how that makes a four-bar force analysis statically indeterminate.
  • Apply the Newton–Euler equations and recognize how they collapse for planar motion.
  • Decide when to do a static force analysis versus an inertia force analysis.
  • Analyze the forces in friction-drive and positive-drive (gear) rolling contacts, including the role of preload and the pressure angle.

The 30-second picture

Every link in a moving machine obeys two vector statements — one for translation, one for rotation. Hold these in your head; the entire chapter is bookkeeping built on top of them:

ΣF = m·ag   ·   ΣMg = Ig·α force → translation · moment → rotation

In the inverse problem the right-hand sides are known numbers (you computed ag and α kinematically). The unknowns are hiding inside ΣF and ΣM — the bearing reactions and the driving torque. Solve the linear system and you’ve sized the machine.

Foundations

0 · The mechanics you’ll lean on

This chapter is Newtonian rigid-body dynamics applied to linkages. Before the machine-specific material, let’s sharpen five ideas from statics and dynamics. If they’re second nature, skim; if not, firm them up now — everything that follows is built on them.

Newton’s second law, for a rigid body

A particle obeys F = m a. A rigid body needs two statements: the net force drives the acceleration of its mass center G, and the net moment about G drives its angular acceleration.

ΣF = m ag     ΣMg = Ig α
Think of it like…

…steering a canoe. Push on the dead center of the canoe and it slides straight — pure translation, the F = ma part. Push on one end and it both slides and spins — you’ve added a moment. A rigid body always tracks both effects at once: where the mass center goes, and how the body turns about it. Two equations, because there are two independent things a body can do.

Moment of a force (the cross product)

The moment of a force F about a point is M = r × F, where r points from the reference point to anywhere on the force’s line of action. In the plane this collapses to a single signed scalar:

M = rxFy − ryFx   (the Z-component — the only one that survives in planar motion)

This is exactly why, although the machine looks two-dimensional, the moments live along the Z axis (out of the page). Hold that thought — it is the seed of §5.2.

Mass moment of inertia Ig

Ig measures how a body’s mass is spread out about an axis through G: mass far from the axis resists angular acceleration far more than mass near it. It is the rotational analog of mass.

Think of it like…

…a figure skater’s spin. Arms out, the same muscles produce a slow spin; arms tucked in, a fast one. The skater didn’t change mass — only how far that mass sits from the spin axis, i.e. Ig. In a machine, a chunky flywheel and a slender rod can weigh the same yet have wildly different Ig, and therefore need wildly different torques to spin up.

Two-force and three-force members

A pinned link carrying loads at only two points (and no torque) is a two-force member: its force must act along the line connecting the two pins. This single fact removes unknowns from a free body diagram instantly — the direction of that link’s force is already known. You’ll use it in Example 5.3 to recognize link 4.

In the real world

Connecting rods, tie rods, and the struts of a bridge truss are all two-force members. The reason a car’s tie rod is a slim straight bar is precisely this: engineers know its force runs end-to-end, so the part only ever sees pure tension or compression — the cheapest, lightest loading to design against.

Solving a determinate linear system

An IDP ends as a set of simultaneous linear equations: so many scalar equilibrium equations, so many unknown force/torque components. The problem is solvable (determinate) only when the count of independent equations equals the count of unknowns. Counting them is a recurring move in this chapter — and when the counts don’t match, that mismatch is itself telling you something physical (see §5.2).

In the real world

This “count the equations, count the unknowns” discipline is the same check a structural engineer runs before trusting a truss analysis, and the same logic a solver like MATLAB or ANSYS applies internally before it will invert a stiffness matrix. Get the count wrong and the math either has no answer or infinitely many — both useless for buying a real motor.

Two Kinds of Problem

Inverse vs. forward dynamics

As hinted back in Chapter 1, every dynamics problem is one of two types. The whole of Chapter 5 lives in the first row of this table; later chapters and most simulation software live in the second.

The two dynamics problems
Problem typeKnown (given)To be determinedTypical use
Inverse Dynamics Problem (IDP)state of motionforces & torquessizing a motor, selecting bearings, stress-checking links
Forward Dynamics Problem (FDP)forces & torquesstate of motionsimulation / prediction — “what will it do?”

Notice the columns are simply swapped. That symmetry is real, but the two problems feel very different to solve. The IDP is algebraic: plug the known accelerations into Newton’s laws and solve a linear system — which is exactly why this chapter is tractable by hand. The FDP is generally a set of coupled differential equations that you integrate forward in time, which is why it usually goes to a computer.

Think of it like…

…a thermostat versus a weather forecast. The IDP is the thermostat: you decide the temperature you want (the motion), and it computes how much heat (force/torque) to deliver right now — a direct calculation. The FDP is the forecast: given today’s conditions (the forces), predict tomorrow’s weather (the resulting motion) — you must march the equations forward through time, and small errors compound.

In the real world

A robot arm uses both, constantly. Its controller solves an IDP thousands of times a second — “to follow this trajectory, command these joint torques” (this is literally called inverse dynamics control). Meanwhile the engineers who designed it ran FDP simulations beforehand — “if the motors saturate, will the arm overshoot and hit something?” Same machine, two complementary questions.

Section 5.1

5.1 · Review of planar kinetics

Start with one link, isolated. Newton’s second law gives the force balance, and the moment balance about the mass center G gives the rotation balance. In the textbook these are Equations 5.1 and 5.4:

ΣF = ΣFei = m ag (5.1)
ΣMg = Ig α (5.4)

Because the links move in the XY plane, the force and acceleration vectors have only X and Y components — their Z component is zero:

Fei = [ Feix ; Feiy ; 0 ]    ag = [ agx ; agy ; 0 ]

So Equation 5.1 is really two scalar equations (one in X, one in Y). The moment of each force about G is the cross product r(e/g) × Fei, and the only surviving component is along Z (Equation 5.5):

Mg = r(e/g)xFeiy − r(e/g)yFeix (5.5)
The tally that matters

Each planar link hands you three scalar equilibrium equations: ΣFx, ΣFy, and ΣMz. Memorize that number — the entire static-determinacy bookkeeping of this chapter is built by multiplying “3 per link” and comparing to the count of unknown force/torque components.

Think of it like…

…a household budget that has to balance in three ledgers at once. Money in must equal money out horizontally (ΣFx), vertically (ΣFy), and in “spin” (ΣM). A link in steady motion is paying its inertia “bills” — m ag and Igα — and the external forces are the income that must cover them, in all three ledgers simultaneously.

Tool 2 · One link, three equations

Watch the inertia “demand” build up

A single link of mass m and inertia Ig is driven through a known motion. Slide its mass-center acceleration and angular acceleration and watch the required net force m ag and net moment Igα grow. In an IDP these are the numbers the bearings and motor must supply.

Set ag = 0 and α = 0 and both demands vanish — that is the special case of a static force analysis (§5.3), where the link isn’t accelerating at all.

Section 5.1 · continued

Moments about an arbitrary point

You are not forced to sum moments about the mass center. Often summing about a different point — usually a pin joint where unknown reactions live — makes those unknowns vanish from the moment equation, leaving fewer unknowns to juggle. Summing about an arbitrary point A (textbook Equation 5.10):

ΣMa = Igα + ( rg/a × m ag )z (5.10)

The extra term is the moment of the inertia force m ag about A. If you sum about G itself, then rg/a = 0 and the term disappears — recovering Equation 5.4. Choosing A wisely is a tactical decision, not a new law.

Think of it like…

…choosing where to stand when you photograph a crowd. The crowd is the same; but stand in the right spot and a distracting lamppost lines up perfectly behind someone and disappears from the shot. Summing moments about a clever point A “hides” an unknown bearing reaction the same way — the physics is unchanged, but your equation is suddenly far cleaner.

In the real world

Practicing engineers almost never sum moments about the mass center first. They sum about a pin — the reaction force there passes through the point, so it contributes zero moment and drops out. One well-chosen reference point can turn a messy three-unknown equation into a one-line solve for the driving torque. It’s the single most useful trick in hand force-analysis.

Tool 3 · Pick your pivot

Make an unknown disappear

A link is pinned at A (left) and carries an unknown reaction there, plus a known applied force at the far end. Choose which point to sum moments about. Sum about A and the unknown pin reaction contributes zero moment — the equation solves immediately. Sum about G and the reaction stays in, so you need a second equation.

Section 5.2

5.2 · Why a “planar” machine is partially three-dimensional

Here is a subtlety that trips up almost everyone. For a planar mechanism, each link’s mass center moves only in X and Y, and each link spins only about Z. So the force analysis ought to be purely 2-D… and yet the chapter insists it is three-dimensional. Where does the third dimension sneak in?

Partially three-dimensional planar mechanism. A mechanism whose links all move in parallel XY planes — not the same plane. Their mass centers are offset along Z (Figure 5.11). The motion is planar; the geometry of the forces is not.

Real links are stacked side-by-side on their shared pins, like the leaves of a hinge. Each link’s mass center sits at a slightly different Z. When a pin transmits force between two links lying in different Z-planes, that force — combined with its Z offset — produces moments about the X and Y axes, not just Z. The machine moves in a plane, but it must be held together in 3-D.

Think of it like…

…a door hinge. The door swings in one flat arc — pure planar motion. But the hinge has several knuckles stacked along the pin, each at a different height. The pin doesn’t just resist the door swinging; it also resists the door twisting and sagging out of plane, because the knuckles push on it at different heights. Those out-of-plane reactions are the “extra” three-dimensional part of a problem that looks two-dimensional.

The consequence: a four-bar is statically indeterminate

Carry the full 3-D force bookkeeping through a general four-bar (Figure 5.15) and the counts don’t balance. The textbook tallies:

8 + 8 + 1 = 17 unknowns set against 5 equilibrium equations per moving link × 3 moving links = 15. The 17 − 15 = 2 gap is exactly the shortfall in the table.

Two more unknowns than equations ⇒ the problem is statically indeterminate. The resolution is physical: although the four joint axes are nominally all along Z, manufacturing tolerances leave tiny angular misalignments between them. Supplying those known misalignments provides the two missing pieces of information and closes the gap.

In the real world

This is exactly why a four-bar linkage built with cheap, sloppy bearings can bind, overheat, or wear unevenly — the indeterminate out-of-plane loads are real, and they go wherever the misalignment sends them. It’s also why precision machinery uses self-aligning bearings or spherical joints: by allowing the small misalignment instead of fighting it, the designer keeps those parasitic 3-D moments from ever building up.

Tool 5 · The out-of-plane offset

Slide the links apart in Z

Two links share a pin but sit in parallel planes a distance z apart. Drag the offset and watch the in-plane force, acting across that Z gap, generate an out-of-plane moment z × F. At z = 0 it’s a clean 2-D problem; grow z and the 3-D moment that drives indeterminacy appears.

Section 5.2 · continued

The Newton–Euler equations

The fully general (spatial) equations of motion for a rigid body are the Newton–Euler equations (Figure 5.12, Equations 5.16 and 5.18):

ΣF = m ag (5.16)
ΣMg = I α + ω × ( I ω ) (5.18)

Here I is the 3×3 inertia matrix (Equation 5.17), whose diagonal entries are the familiar moments of inertia and whose off-diagonal entries are the products of inertia:

I = [ Ixx  −Ixy  −Ixz ;
  −Iyx   Iyy  −Iyz ;
  −Izx  −Izy   Izz ] (5.17)

The term ω × (Iω) is the gyroscopic term — the reason a spinning top precesses. It looks intimidating, but for a planar machine it almost entirely evaporates.

Collapse to planar motion

A link in planar motion has a plane of symmetry parallel to the plane of motion (Figure 5.13). That symmetry forces the products of inertia Ixz and Iyz to zero, and the angular velocity points purely along Z. Equation 5.18 collapses dramatically (Equation 5.20):

ΣMg = [ 0 ; 0 ; Izzα ] + ( ω × Iω = 0 ) = [ 0 ; 0 ; Igα ] (5.20)

The whole spatial mouthful reduces to the friendly scalar ΣM = Igα you already used in §5.1. That is why we could start there: planar symmetry quietly did the hard work.

Think of it like…

…a perfectly balanced car tire versus a wobbly one. A balanced, symmetric tire spins about its axle with zero shake — no gyroscopic surprises (the products of inertia are zero). An out-of-balance tire has mass off the symmetry plane, so spinning it produces a wobble that fights the axle — that’s the ω×Iω term made physical. Planar machine links are the balanced tire: symmetric about their plane, so the wobble term is exactly zero.

Composite bodies

A link made by welding several shapes together (Figure 5.14) may not be symmetric about a single plane, so the clean collapse (Eq. 5.22) needs the correction terms of Equations 5.28 and 5.29, summing each sub-shape’s contribution. The principle is unchanged — just more bookkeeping for an asymmetric part.

In the real world

The gyroscopic term you got to drop here is the entire design problem for helicopter rotors, ship stabilizers, and reaction wheels on satellites. Planar-machine designers get to ignore it; aerospace designers build their careers on it. Knowing why it vanished here tells you exactly when you’re no longer allowed to ignore it.

Section 5.3

5.3 · Static force analysis vs. inertia force analysis

In any IDP the known loads fall into two buckets: externally applied forces and torques (weights, springs, the load the machine is working against) and inertia loads (the m ag and Igα terms). Frequently one bucket dominates the other, and you can simplify by neglecting the smaller one. That gives two limiting analyses:

Static force analysis

External loads dominate; inertia is negligible. Formally, set every mass and inertia to zero (all m’s and I’s → 0), so m ag = 0 and Igα = 0. Every link is in ordinary static equilibrium.

Use when: the machine moves slowly, or the working load dwarfs the part weights.

Inertia force analysis

Inertia dominates; some external loads (often weight, sometimes the load torque) are negligible by comparison. The m ag and Igα terms are the main event.

Use when: the machine runs fast, with light parts and large accelerations.

Both are still the IDP

Neither is a new method — they’re just the full IDP with one bucket of terms dropped because it’s small. When in doubt, keep everything: a full dynamic analysis carries both buckets and is always correct. The simplifications are about effort, not principle.

Think of it like…

…weighing a letter on a postal scale versus a kitchen scale during an earthquake. Sitting still, the only thing that matters is the letter’s weight — that’s static analysis. But if you’re jiggling the scale violently up and down, the reading is dominated by how fast you’re shaking it (the inertia), and the actual weight barely registers — that’s inertia analysis. Same object; which effect dominates depends entirely on how hard it’s being accelerated.

In the real world

A hand-cranked can opener turns slowly — pure static analysis sizes it fine. The connecting rod in a 7000-rpm motorcycle engine is the opposite extreme: at top speed the inertia force flinging the rod around is thousands of times its own weight, so gravity is rounding error and the analysis is all inertia. The same chapter of theory covers both; only the dominant bucket changes.

Section 5.4

5.4 · Force analysis of rolling contacts

Back in Chapter 3 you met two ways for one body to roll on another. Now we analyze the forces at the contact. The two kinds behave very differently:

Friction drive

Two smooth wheels pressed together. Motion is transmitted only by static friction at the contact. The no-slip condition holds only while there’s enough normal force.

Positive drive

Gear teeth (involute profile) interlock. Motion is transmitted by the teeth pushing on each other — no reliance on friction, so it can never slip.

Compliance: why a single contact point is a fiction

In theory two rolling bodies touch at a single point. In practice the arms that carry them have length errors. Too long and the bodies overlap (can’t even assemble); too short and there’s a gap — no normal force, so no friction, so no drive. The fix is compliance: a built-in spring (link 4 in Figure 5.28) that takes up the slack and presses the wheels together regardless of small length errors.

Preload. The normal force Fn23 created by the compliant element. It must be large enough to guarantee the no-slip condition through static friction:

Ft23 ≤ μstatic Fn23 no-slip condition for a friction drive

If the tangential (drive) force ever exceeds μ Fn, the wheels slip and the drive fails. The preload spring is what keeps Fn high enough.

Think of it like…

…pinching a sheet of paper to pull it from a stack. Pinch too lightly (low preload) and your fingers just slide — the paper stays put. Pinch hard enough and friction grips, and the paper comes with your hand. The harder the pull you need (tangential force), the harder you must pinch (normal preload). A friction drive’s spring is doing the pinching for you, automatically and constantly.

In the real world

Older record players and tape decks used a spring-loaded rubber idler wheel — a friction drive with built-in compliance — to spin the platter at a steady speed. When the spring weakened or the rubber hardened, the preload dropped, the wheel slipped, and the music slowed and wowed. That audible failure is the no-slip condition being violated.

Positive drive: the pressure angle

Gears with involute teeth give a positive drive with three elegant properties (Figure 5.30): the common normal’s direction never changes (the pressure angle Φ is constant), the virtual contact point (the pitch point P) doesn’t move, and the common normal always passes through P. The contact force therefore splits cleanly into a tangential part (which does the driving) and a normal part, related by the pressure angle (Equation 5.43):

tanΦ = Fn32 / Ft32  →  Fn32 = Ft32 tanΦ (5.43)

The tangential component is the useful, torque-producing force. The normal component is “along for the ride” — it does no work but it does push the gears apart and load their bearings. A larger pressure angle means a larger separating force for the same drive.

Think of it like…

…pushing a child on a swing from slightly the wrong angle. If you push perfectly along the swing’s motion, every bit of your effort adds speed (all tangential). Push at an angle and only part of your effort drives the swing; the rest just shoves the child sideways against the chains — wasted, and hard on the hardware. The pressure angle Φ is exactly that “wrong angle” baked into the gear geometry: tanΦ sets how much of the tooth force is the useless sideways shove.

In the real world

Standard industrial gears use a 20° pressure angle — a deliberate compromise. A smaller angle wastes less force on separation and runs quieter, but makes thin, weak teeth that chip. A larger angle gives stout, strong teeth but heavier bearing loads and more noise. That single number on a gear catalog is a force-analysis decision straight out of Equation 5.43.

Example 5.4 · What if it were a friction drive instead?

Same mechanism, but suppose the contact between 3 and 5 were a friction drive rather than gears. What changes in the force/moment bookkeeping?

The free body diagrams are identical — so what actually changes?

The FBDs and the count of unknowns are exactly the same as Example 5.3. The difference is one equation:

  • In the positive drive, the extra equation tying the contact force together is the pressure-angle relation Fn35 = Ft35 tanΦ (Eq. 5.48) — giving 10 equations in 10 unknowns.
  • In a friction drive, Equation 5.48 no longer applies. Instead the force in the arm, F4, is a known preload. That removes one unknown, reducing the system to 9 equations in 9 unknowns.

Both are determinate — just balanced differently. The text notes the practical punchline: a friction drive would realistically not be used in this mechanism, and in general friction drives are less common than positive drives. The wiper’s gears are the right call.

The transferable lesson

Whenever you change a joint’s type, you change which extra equation closes the system — not the free body diagram. Always re-run the count: a determinate problem has equations = unknowns, every time.

In the real world

This is exactly the calculation an engine designer runs — except they sweep θ2 through all 360° to find the worst-case bearing load and the peak torque, which set the bearing size and the flywheel. Notice the scale: at ~1910 rpm the bearing forces reach several kilonewtons on parts weighing a couple of kilograms — the inertia force is hundreds of times the weight. That is why gravity was safely dropped, and why high-revving engines live or die by how light their reciprocating parts are.

Sweep the crank and watch the loads

The same mechanism as Example 5.5, solved live at every crank angle. Drag θ2 and the speed; the linkage redraws and the driving torque and three pin reactions update. Spin it slowly to find where the torque peaks and where a bearing sees its worst load — the numbers a designer actually sizes against.

Set ω2 = 0 and every inertia load collapses to zero — with no external load left, the torque and reactions vanish. That is the static limit (§5.3) of this very machine.

Objectives Recap

What you should now be able to do

  • Distinguish the inverse dynamics problem (motion → forces) from the forward problem (forces → motion), and identify which a design task is asking.
  • Write the three planar equilibrium equations per link, summing moments about G or about a strategically chosen arbitrary point A.
  • Encode known and unknown loads with the standard FBD notation, and count unknowns against equations to test determinacy.
  • Explain why a planar machine is partially three-dimensional and why a 3-D four-bar force analysis is statically indeterminate (17 vs. 15) until misalignments are specified.
  • Apply the Newton–Euler equations and show how planar symmetry kills the gyroscopic term, recovering ΣM = Igα.
  • Choose between static and inertia force analysis based on which loads dominate.
  • Analyze friction-drive (preload, no-slip Ft ≤ μFn) and positive-drive (Fn = Ft tanΦ) rolling contacts.
