What you'll study

Course Description

The design approach is applied to machines such as the cam and follower, speed changers, geared transmissions, planetary gear systems, and linkages for generating a specific type of motion. Graphic, analytical, and digital computer methods are used throughout.

Analogy · The relay team

A machine is a team of rigid parts (called links) passing motion from one to the next like runners in a relay race — each joint is the baton hand-off. Kinematics studies where and how fast the baton travels; dynamics asks how hard each runner must push to keep it moving. This course teaches you to predict both before a single part is machined.

Real-world · You're surrounded by this
  • Cam & follower — the lobes on an engine camshaft open and close valves with split-second timing.
  • Planetary gear systems — the heart of an automatic transmission and of e-bike and power-tool hub gears.
  • Linkages — a car hood hinge, a folding chair, a backhoe arm, and a bicycle drivetrain are all linkages designed for a "specific type of motion."
  • Geared transmissions & speed changers — every drill, mixer, and wind-turbine nacelle trades speed for torque with gears.

The three methods, and why all three

Engineers rarely trust a single tool. A graphical construction gives fast intuition and a sanity check you can sketch by hand; an analytical (vector/equation) model gives exact, general answers; and a digital computer model lets you sweep an entire range of motion in seconds. You'll practice moving fluidly between them.

Analogy · Three maps of the same city

Think of the graphical, analytical, and computer methods as a paper map, a written set of directions, and live GPS. Each describes the same streets, but you reach for a different one depending on whether you need the big picture, an exact route, or a thousand routes at once. Knowing all three makes you hard to get lost.

Required materials

Textbook & Materials

Required textbook

Mechanisms and Machines: Kinematics, Dynamics, and Synthesis, 1st edition.
Michael M. Stanisic. Cengage Learning, 2015. ISBN-13 978-1-133-94391-4 · ISBN-10 1-133-94391-8.

This text is built around the vector loop method and kinematic coefficients — the same two tools the interactive modules use — and it deliberately teaches synthesis first, then analysis, so you learn to design a mechanism before taking one apart.

Software · MATLAB

This course also uses MATLAB. Students are provided free MATLAB access: you'll receive an email at your Brookdale address (from MathWorks) with instructions for downloading and installing it.

If the email hasn't arrived by the first week, check your Brookdale spam folder, then contact your instructor. A short primer is linked under Topics & Modules.

Additional time requirements

None other than completing class assignments.

Real-world · Why a "vector approach" matters

Industry kinematics software (the motion solvers inside CAD packages and robotics toolkits) is built on exactly the vector-loop and kinematic-coefficient ideas in this text. Learning the method by hand and in MATLAB — and practicing synthesis before analysis, the way the book is organized — is what lets you trust, and debug, the software later.

Analogy · A four-cylinder engine

Picture your grade as a four-cylinder engine: the two exams, your quizzes, and your homework each fire one power stroke worth 25%. No single cylinder carries the engine, so one weak stroke doesn't stall you — but the ride is only smooth when all four fire steadily. Skipping homework or coasting on quizzes is like running on three cylinders: the engine still turns over, just rough and down on power.

The "C or higher" gate

You must earn a grade of C or higher before taking any course for which ENGI 216 is a prerequisite. The C line (70%) is marked with the orange rule in the table.

Analogy · The clutch engagement threshold

A clutch doesn't transmit any torque to the next stage of a drivetrain until it's pressed past a certain engagement point. The "C or higher" rule is that engagement threshold: reach it, and the next course in your sequence engages; fall short, and the drive doesn't transfer until you do.
