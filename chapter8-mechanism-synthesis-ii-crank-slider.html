Welcome back

From rotation-to-rotation to rotation-to-translation

Last week you learned to design a four-bar to deliver a desired angular output from an angular input. This week we do the same thing for the crank-slider, where the output is now a translation — the position of a sliding block — instead of a rotation. The whole conceptual toolkit carries over: vector loop, Freudenstein's equation, point-matching, derivative-matching. Only the names change, and one critical property doesn't carry over: scale.

Function generation for a crank-slider. Determining the link lengths r1, r2, r3 of a crank-slider mechanism so that the slider translation r is a prescribed function of the crank rotation φ: r = f(φ).

What you'll be able to do after this module

  • State and use the crank-slider Freudenstein equation (Eq. 8.35) and recognize the role of the three S quantities.
  • Explain — and demonstrate with the comparator tool — why scale matters now when it didn't for the four-bar.
  • Synthesize a crank-slider by point-matching: three precision points, 3×3 linear solve, three link lengths.
  • Synthesize a crank-slider by derivative-matching: one design position, four matched derivatives, with two genuinely distinct mechanism solutions.
  • Reproduce the two worked examples in this module — a linear-advance design (point-matching) and a cam-replacement design (derivative-matching).

Four-bar synthesis, the essentials

  • Freudenstein's equation rewrote the four-bar loop in three dimensionless ratios R1, R2, R3, linear in those ratios — so three precision points give a 3×3 system.
  • Point-matching: hit three (φ, ψ) precision points exactly. Lagrange-interpolation analog. Free choices: θ2i, θ4i, one link length.

  • Scale was free. The Freudenstein ratios are dimensionless — doubling all four link lengths didn't change the input-output curve. That's the part that's about to change.

If you skip the recap, here's the one fact you need

In Part I, the three Freudenstein ratios R1 = r1/r4, R2 = r1/r2, R3 = …/(2 r2 r4) were all dimensionless. Now in Part II, the analogous quantities S1 = 2 r2, S2 = 2 r1 r2, S3 = r3² − r2² − r1² are dimensioned (linear length, area, area). That single fact drives everything different about Part II.

Foundations

The crank-slider geometry

A crank-slider has three links plus a sliding joint. Numbering them the way the textbook does:

  • Link 1 — the ground frame. The crank pivots from a point we'll call O2. The slider runs along a straight track that's offset from O2 by a distance r1 (Stanišić's Figure 8.14 shows the track above the pivot; r1 is the perpendicular distance).
  • Link 2 — the crank. Length r2, rotates about O2. Its orientation is θ2.
  • Link 3 — the coupler. Length r3, connects the crank tip to the slider.
  • Link 4 — the slider, which is treated as a translating link. The position of the slider along its track is r4 — a length, not an angle.

The loop equation

The vector loop is structurally identical to the four-bar loop, but now r4 changes magnitude rather than direction:

(8.1, again) r⃗2 + r⃗3 − r⃗4 − r⃗1 = 0⃗

Picking components along and perpendicular to the slider track, squaring, and eliminating the coupler angle θ3 the same way Part I did — but with the slider geometry — produces Eq. 8.35 below.

Angles and rotations

Same bookkeeping conventions as Part I: θ2 is the absolute crank orientation, φ is the rotation from a chosen initial pose θ2i, and now r is the slider's translation from a chosen initial position r4i:

(8.37, 8.38) φ = θ2 − θ2i     r = r4 − r4i
Think of it like…

If the four-bar was a watch hand driving another watch hand, the crank-slider is a watch hand driving a cursor on a ruler. Rotation in, translation out. The driving math is nearly identical because both are described by vector loops — only the kind of joint at the output changes.

In the real world

The crank-slider is the most-used mechanism on the planet. Every internal-combustion engine is a crank-slider — the piston (slider) drives the crankshaft (crank) via the connecting rod (coupler). Every reciprocating compressor, pump, and steam engine is a crank-slider. Sewing machines, jackhammers, oil-well pumpjacks, and the slide on a bolt-action rifle — all crank-sliders. The reason this matters is that crank-slider synthesis isn't a curiosity; it's how engines and pumps get designed.

§ 8.2 · The Key Equation

Freudenstein's equation for the crank-slider

The same derivation pattern as Part I, applied to the slider geometry, gives the crank-slider Freudenstein equation. It's structurally analogous but importantly different:

(8.35) · Freudenstein for crank-slider S1 r4 cosθ2 + S2 sinθ2 + S3 = r4²

Where the three S quantities are:

(8.36) S1 = 2r2    S2 = 2r1r2    S3 = r3² − r2² − r1²
Show the derivation (4 steps)

Step 1. With the crank pivot at the origin, slider track along the line y = r1, and crank tip at A = (r2cosθ2, r2sinθ2), the slider point B = (r4, r1). The coupler constraint says |A − B| = r3.

Step 2. Square that constraint:

(r4 − r2cosθ2)² + (r1 − r2sinθ2)² = r3²

Step 3. Expand and rearrange to isolate the r4² term on the right:

r4² = 2r2cosθ2 · r4 + 2r1r2sinθ2 + (r3² − r2² − r1²)

Step 4. Group the link-length expressions into S1, S2, S3. That's Eq. 8.35.

Compare with Part I, line by line

Four-bar Freudenstein had cos(θ2 − θ4) on the right because two angles needed eliminating; crank-slider Freudenstein has r4² on the right because one of the "angles" was replaced by a length. The structure is the same: three quantities (R's there, S's here) linear in the equation, three precision points needed to nail them.

The crucial difference: dimensions

In Part I, R1, R2, R3 were all dimensionless ratios. Here, the S's all have dimensions:

That's the algebraic shadow of a deep geometric fact: a crank-slider's input-output curve depends on the actual physical size of its links, not just on their proportions. We're about to make that visible.

The Big Difference

Scale doesn't matter for a four-bar. It does for a crank-slider.

This is the most important conceptual point in Part II. Let me make it visible with a side-by-side experiment.

Think of it like…

Imagine two identical Lego car designs. The four-bar mechanism is like a scale model of a car — make the model twice as big and it still steers the same way (same turning radius proportions, same gear ratios). The crank-slider is more like an actual engine: make every part twice as big and the piston travels a different fraction of its bore per crank degree — because the bore distance itself has changed in a way the input angle doesn't know about.

Why algebraically?

In the four-bar Freudenstein equation, if you replace every ri with c·ri (multiply all by some constant c), each R is unchanged because numerator and denominator both pick up the same factor. The equation is invariant; the input-output curve is unchanged.

In the crank-slider Freudenstein equation, the same substitution gives S1 → c S1, S2 → c² S2, S3 → c² S3, and r4 → c r4. The S terms grow at different rates (c and c²), so the equation is not invariant under uniform scaling. The curve changes.

In the real world

Why does this matter? In a real engine, the stroke (slider travel) and bore-to-stroke ratio are critical design quantities affecting torque, RPM range, fuel efficiency, and emissions. Doubling every link length doubles the stroke. The function generation problem for a crank-slider has to specify actual lengths, not just ratios — because the customer is asking for "this much piston travel," not "some unspecified amount times some scale."

§ 8.2.1 · Method 1

Point-matching for the crank-slider

Just as in Part I, three precision points fully determine the three unknowns in Freudenstein's equation. The precision points are now (φk, rk) pairs — an input rotation and a desired output translation. Convert to (θ2,k, r4,k) using your chosen θ2i and r4i, then write Eq. 8.35 three times:

(8.39) 
[ (r4)1 cosθ2,1   sinθ2,1   1 ] [S1]   [ (r4)1² ]
[ (r4)2 cosθ2,2   sinθ2,2   1 ] [S2] = [ (r4)2² ]
[ (r4)3 cosθ2,3   sinθ2,3   1 ] [S3]   [ (r4)3² ]

Recovering the link lengths

After solving for S1, S2, S3, back out the three link lengths from Eq. 8.36 in order:

  1. r2 = S1/2 — the crank length is half S1.
  2. r1 = S2/(2 r2) — the offset, once r2 is known.
  3. r3 = √(S3 + r1² + r2²) — the coupler, from the definition of S3.
No "assume one length" here

In Part I, you had to assume one link length (typically r1) to set the scale, because the three R's only determined three ratios. In Part II, the three S's directly determine all three link lengths — no assumption needed. That's the upside of scale mattering: you get the actual sizes for free, but you also have to specify your precision points in actual length units (inches, mm, feet — not just ratios).

Think of it like…

Point-matching for a four-bar was Lagrange interpolation for angles; point-matching for a crank-slider is Lagrange interpolation for a distance-vs-angle curve. Same algebra, same solve, same "exact at three points, drift between them" behavior. The difference is the curve being interpolated has mixed units — degrees on the x-axis, length on the y-axis.

§ 8.2.2 · Method 2

Derivative-matching for the crank-slider

The Part I idea translates cleanly: instead of three precision points, match a single design position together with the first three derivatives of the desired output. Now those derivatives are of r (a length) with respect to φ (a rotation), so their dimensions are length, length, length again:

(8.40-style) f4 = dr4/dθ2 = dr/dφ     f′4 = d²r/dφ²     f″4 = d³r/dφ³

All three are in length units (in/rad, or whatever unit r uses divided by radian). Compute them by differentiating your desired function r = f(φ) three times and evaluating at the design position.

The supplementary tower

Following the same conceptual pattern as Part I — but with new formulas reflecting the slider geometry — derivative-matching builds nine intermediate quantities Di, Ei, Fi directly from the targets f4, f′4, f″4 and the design value of r4des:

(8.50) E1 = f4    D1 = −r4    F1 = 2 r4 f4
E2 = r4 − f′4    D2 = 2 f4    F2 = −2(f4² + r4 f′4)
E3 = 3 f4 − f″4    D3 = 3 f′4 − r4    F3 = −2(3 f4 f′4 + r4 f″4)

The quadratic in tan θ2

Just like Part I, derivative-matching reduces to a quadratic:

(8.53) λ1 tan²θ2 + λ2 tanθ2 + λ3 = 0
(8.54) λ1 = F1D3 − F3D1
λ2 = F1E3 − F3E1 + D1F2 − D2F1 + F3D2 − F2D3
λ3 = E1F2 − E2F1 + F3E2 − F2E3

Finishing up

With a chosen tan-root determining θ2des, the S's follow from:

(8.51, 8.52) S1 = G1/G    S2 = G2/G
G1 = F1sinθ2 − F2cosθ2
G2 = (E1cosθ2 + D1sinθ2)F2 − (E2cosθ2 + D2sinθ2)F1
G = (E1cosθ2 + D1sinθ2)sinθ2 − (E2cosθ2 + D2sinθ2)cosθ2

And S3 drops out of Eq. 8.35 evaluated at the design pair. Link lengths follow from Eq. 8.36 just as in point-matching.

Two real solutions, not one

The quadratic in tan θ2 typically gives two distinct real roots, and unlike Part I's Example 8.2 (where both roots produced the same mechanism), the crank-slider's two roots often produce two genuinely different mechanisms that both match the same target derivatives. The Example B walkthrough below shows one such pair. This is design freedom you can use to pick the more compact, less awkward solution.

§ 8.2.3 · The Checklist

The 11-step crank-slider derivative-matching procedure

Stanišić's procedure for the crank-slider mirrors the four-bar procedure step-for-step. Walk through these in order:

  1. Choose a design position. Pick (φdes, rdes) satisfying the desired f(φ, r) = 0.
  2. Compute the three derivative targets f4, f′4, f″4 by differentiating the desired r(φ) three times and evaluating at φdes.
  3. Assume a value for r4des (a free choice).
  4. Compute Di, Ei, Fi for i = 1, 2, 3 from Eq. 8.50.
  5. Compute λ1, λ2, λ3 from Eq. 8.54.
  6. Solve the quadratic Eq. 8.53 for two values of tan θ2. Keep either; both lead to valid designs.
  7. Pick θ2des as atan(tan θ2) from your chosen root.
  8. Compute G, G1, G2 from Eq. 8.52, then S1, S2 from Eq. 8.51.
  9. Compute S3 from Eq. 8.35 evaluated at (θ2des, r4des).
  10. Compute link lengths r2 = S1/2, r1 = S2/(2 r2), r3 = √(S3 + r1² + r2²).
  11. Compute initial values θ2i = θ2des − φdes and r4i = r4des − rdes. The mechanism is now fully defined.

In the real world

Derivative-matching a crank-slider is the right approach when you care about local kinematic quality near the operating point — a quiet running engine wants smooth piston acceleration through top-dead-center; a pneumatic press wants near-constant velocity through the workpiece; a printer carriage wants zero jerk at the page-edge reversal. Pick the operating point, compute the three derivative targets from your desired motion law, and derivative-match.

Example A — Linear-advance design (point-matching)

The problem

Replace a 4-inch lead screw with a crank-slider that approximates uniform linear advance over a 90° crank sweep. The lead screw is being phased out for noise reasons (it's part of a desktop 3D-printer mechanism); we want a crank-slider that gives essentially the same r(φ) but with cleaner bearings and no thread wear.

Desired function: r = (4 in / 90°) · φ, i.e., r(φ) = (4/90)·φ, over φ ∈ [0°, 90°].

Step 4 · Back out link lengths from Eq. 8.36

r2 = S1/2 ≈ −2.744 in
r1 = S2/(2 r2) ≈ −8.226 / (−5.489) ≈ +1.499 in

The sign on r2 is negative: physically this means the crank starts pointing in the opposite direction from our assumed θ2i = 60°. Take the magnitude (2.744 in) and read the crank as pointing 180° + 60° = 240° at the start. The mechanism is fully valid; just flip that orientation in your drawing.

Final magnitudes: r1 ≈ 1.50 in, r2 ≈ 2.74 in, r3 ≈ 13.92 in. An offset slider (offset 1.5 in above the pivot), a short crank, and a long coupler — exactly the proportions of a typical reciprocating mechanism.

Step 5 · Verify with Tool 2

Open Tool 2 (or click "Load Example A") and verify the numbers. The actual slider position will land exactly on the desired line at the three precision points; in between, it'll deviate by a fraction of an inch — the unavoidable interpolation error.

Example B — Cam-replacement design (derivative-matching)

The problem

An existing plate-feed cam in an automated transfer line is being retired. At the design crank angle, the cam delivers slider position r4 ≈ 11.44 in with first three derivatives (with respect to crank angle): dr4/dθ2 = −2.764 in/rad, d²r4/dθ2² = −1.442 in/rad², d³r4/dθ2³ = 3.943 in/rad³. Find a crank-slider that matches all three derivatives — so the new mechanism behaves locally just like the cam.

Design target: match (f4, f′4, f″4) = (−2.764, −1.442, 3.943) at r4des = 11.44 in.

Step 1 · Choose design position & compute targets

Use the design position handed to us: φdes = 0 (the cam's operating angle, set as our zero reference), rdes = 0 (slider's zero is the cam's mid-range), r4des = 11.44 in. Targets given.

Step 2 · Compute D, E, F from Eq. 8.50

E1 = f4 = −2.764    D1 = −r4 = −11.44    F1 = 2 r4 f4 = −63.24
E2 = r4 − f′4 = 12.88    D2 = 2 f4 = −5.528    F2 = −2(f4² + r4f′4) = 17.71
E3 = 3 f4 − f″4 = −12.23    D3 = 3 f′4 − r4 = −15.77    F3 = −2(3 f4 f′4 + r4 f″4) = −66.31

Step 3 · Lambdas and the quadratic

λ1 = F1D3 − F3D1 ≈ −308.60
λ2 ≈ 816.15
λ3 ≈ −487.77

Solving λ1 tan²θ2 + λ2 tan θ2 + λ3 = 0:

tan θ2 = 0.9125   (root +√, gives θ2 ≈ 42.4°)
tan θ2 = 1.7322   (root −√, gives θ2 ≈ 60.0°)

Both lead to physical mechanisms. We'll work out both.

Step 4 · Mechanism A — root −√ (θ2des ≈ 60°)

G ≈ 32.00    G1 ≈ 192.0    G2 ≈ 288.1
S1 = G1/G ≈ 6.000    S2 = G2/G ≈ 9.003
S3 from Eq. 8.35 evaluated at design point ≈ 88.75

Link lengths from Eq. 8.36:

r2 = S1/2 ≈ 3.000 in
r1 = S2/(2 r2) ≈ 1.500 in
r3 = √(88.75 + 2.25 + 9.00) ≈ 10.00 in

Clean round numbers. This is the "obvious" solution.

Step 5 · Mechanism B — root +√ (θ2des ≈ 42.4°)

S1 ≈ 5.134    S2 ≈ −17.82    S3 ≈ 99.49

r2 = S1/2 ≈ 2.567 in
r1 = S2/(2 r2) ≈ −3.470 in   (sign-flip; magnitude 3.47 in)
r3 = √(99.49 + 12.04 + 6.59) ≈ 10.87 in

A completely different mechanism — different crank length, different offset (in the opposite direction!), different coupler — but it matches the same three derivatives at the same design position. Locally, the two mechanisms produce indistinguishable slider motion. Globally (away from the design point), they diverge.

Think of it like…

The quadratic gives two designs the same way two pencils touching a curve at the same point with the same slope, curvature, and rate-of-change-of-curvature can still be different pencils. Locally identical, globally distinct. In four-bar synthesis (Part I, Example 8.2) the two roots collapsed because of the symmetric ψ = −φ + 10° function; here, no such collapse, and the designer gets a genuine choice.

Step 6 · Verify with Tool 3

Open Tool 3 and click "Load Example B." Toggle between the two tan-roots and watch the mechanism change while the desired-vs-actual curve at the design point stays tangent to the desired curve. The two mechanisms diverge away from the design point — that's the global-vs-local trade-off in action.
