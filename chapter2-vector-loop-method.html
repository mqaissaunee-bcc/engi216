Orientation

What this chapter is about

In Chapter 1 you learned to recognize a mechanism and to count its degrees of freedom. You could say whether a device moves. This chapter answers the much harder question: exactly how does every link move? Given the dimensions of a one-degree-of-freedom mechanism and the motion of its driving link, the vector loop method lets you solve for the position, velocity, and acceleration of every other link and of any point of interest on them.

By the end of this lecture you will be able to…

  • State the kinematic analysis problem for a one-DOF mechanism: what is given, and what you solve for.
  • Attach vectors to a mechanism so they form a closed loop, and write the vector loop equation (VLE).
  • Decompose a VLE into scalar position equations in X and Y.
  • Apply the two vector checks and the loop-counting test to confirm you have assigned good vectors and enough loops.
  • Recognize when a system has a closed-form solution and when it needs a numerical one.
  • Run Newton’s method to solve nonlinear position equations, and read the Jacobian’s role.
  • Locate a point of interest (such as a mass center) using a known vector on a link.

Kinematic analysis — given a mechanism’s dimensions and the motion of one (input) link, find the motion of all other links.
Vector loop equation (VLE) — the statement that a chain of link-vectors forming a closed path sums to zero.
Position equations — the scalar X and Y equations that come from a VLE; they fix the mechanism’s configuration.
Why bother with the position problem at all?

Beginning mechanics courses deal mostly with the velocity and acceleration aspects of a mechanism, because the position part is complex and nonlinear — it is full of sines and cosines of unknown angles. The vector loop method is worth learning precisely because it tackles all three aspects (position, velocity, acceleration) in one consistent framework, and because it is well suited to being put on a computer. Once the position equations are written, the velocity equations are just their first derivatives and the acceleration equations their second derivatives. Solve the hard nonlinear position problem once, and the rest follows by differentiation.

Section 0 · Foundational background

The vector toolkit you need first

The whole method rests on one humble idea: a planar vector can be written two ways, and you must move fluently between them. If this is rusty, fix it now — everything downstream depends on it.

Polar form — magnitude and direction

A vector r̄ of length r pointing at angle θ from the positive X axis. This is how mechanism links naturally present themselves: a link has a fixed length, and its angle changes as the mechanism moves.

r̄ = r ∠ θ

Component form — X and Y projections

The same vector resolved onto the axes. This is the form you can add, subtract, and feed to algebra.

rx = r·cosθ    ry = r·sinθ
Think of it like… giving directions

Polar form is “walk 3 blocks toward the church spire.” Component form is “walk 2 blocks east and 2 blocks north.” Same trip, same endpoint — just two languages for it. People navigate naturally in polar (“that way, about a mile”), but a street grid forces you into components. Vector loop algebra is the same: you see links in polar, but you must compute in components.

In the real world… a backhoe boom

A backhoe arm is a chain of links whose lengths are fixed at the factory but whose angles change every second the operator works. The cab’s hydraulics command angles (polar); the bucket’s actual position on the jobsite is an X–Y location (components). Converting between the two, link by link, is exactly the calculation this chapter builds.

The one habit that prevents most errors

Measure every angle the same way: counter-clockwise from the positive X axis, with the vector’s tail at the point you are measuring from. Mixed conventions are the single biggest source of sign errors in vector loop work.

The closed-loop idea, in one sentence

If you start at a point, walk along several vectors laid head-to-tail, and arrive back where you started, then your net displacement is zero — so the vectors must sum to the zero vector. That is the entire physical content of a vector loop equation. A mechanism is rigid and connected, so the chain of link-vectors around any closed path through it must close on itself; therefore it sums to zero. Each scalar component of that zero (the X part and the Y part) gives one position equation.

Section 2.1

Kinematic Analysis & the Vector Loop Method

The kinematic analysis problem (one-DOF mechanism). Given: the dimensions of the mechanism and the state of motion (position, velocity, acceleration) of one input link. Find: the state of motion of every other link and of any points of interest, such as mass centers.

The first — and most error-prone — task is to assign the vector loop correctly. Once the loop is right, the position equations follow mechanically, and from them the velocity and acceleration equations follow by differentiation. So we spend real care on the loop.

Think of it like… a walk around the block

Leave your front door, turn at each corner, and keep going until you’re back at the door. However many streets you walked, your net displacement is zero — east cancels west, north cancels south. A vector loop equation says exactly that about a mechanism: trace the links around any closed path and the displacements must cancel, so they sum to 0̄. The corners are the joints; the streets are the links.

The four steps that produce position equations

Step 1 — attach vectors forming a closed loop

Put one vector along each link so that, traced head-to-tail, they form a closed polygon. A four-bar linkage gives a four-sided loop: ground, crank, coupler, rocker.

Step 2 — write the vector loop equation

The sum of the loop vectors is the zero vector. With careful sign choices for direction, for a four-bar this reads:

r̄₂ + r̄₃ − r̄₄ − r̄₁ = 0̄

Step 3 — fix the origin

Place the origin of the XY frame at a fixed (ground) point in the loop, and align an axis with a convenient link. Good frame choices make the algebra simpler.

Step 4 — decompose into X and Y

Project the VLE onto the axes. Each vector contributes r·cosθ to the X equation and r·sinθ to the Y equation. One vector equation becomes two scalar position equations.

For the four-bar above, with the origin at the ground pin of link 2 and the X axis along the ground link r̄₁, the two scalar position equations are:

r₂cosθ₂ + r₃cosθ₃ − r₄cosθ₄ − r₁ = 0 X-component
r₂sinθ₂ + r₃sinθ₃ − r₄sinθ₄ = 0 Y-component
In the real world… a windshield wiper

A car’s wiper linkage is a four-bar: a small motor crank spins continuously, the coupler transmits the motion, and the wiper arm (the rocker) sweeps back and forth across the glass. The two scalar equations above are precisely what an engineer solves to guarantee the blade reaches the edge of the windshield but never flies off it. Change one link length and the wipe pattern changes — which is why the solver below lets you do exactly that.

Steps 5 through 8 — knowns, solving, and differentiating

  • Step 5 — list scalar knowns and unknowns. Count them. If the number of unknowns exceeds the number of equations, the mechanism is movable (the excess equals the number of DOF). If unknowns ≤ equations, it is not a mechanism — it is a structure or statically indeterminate.
  • Step 6 — solve for the position unknowns. Closed-form if possible (Section 2.3); otherwise numerically with Newton’s method (Section 2.4).
  • Step 7 — differentiate once to get velocity equations; solve for the velocity unknowns.
  • Step 8 — differentiate again to get acceleration equations; solve for the acceleration unknowns.
The movability test as bookkeeping

Notice how this mirrors Gruebler from Chapter 1. There, F = 3(N−1) − 2P₁ − P₂ counted freedoms structurally. Here you arrive at the same conclusion through the equations themselves: a one-DOF mechanism has exactly one more scalar unknown than it has position equations. Specify the input (one value) and the system becomes solvable.

Think of it like… equations and unknowns as a budget

Counting unknowns against equations is just balancing a checkbook. Every scalar equation is a dollar of “buying power” that can pin down one unknown. If you have three unknowns but only two equations, you are one dollar short — the system is free to move in one direction, and that shortfall is the single degree of freedom. Hand it the one input value it’s missing and the books balance: now every unknown is paid for, and the configuration is fully determined.

Section 2.2

Hints for Choosing Vectors

There are correct and incorrect ways to assign vectors to a mechanism. A bad assignment can introduce phantom unknowns or fail to capture a joint’s real constraint, and the equations that follow will be wrong even though the algebra is flawless. Two checks, applied to every vector, keep you honest.

Think of it like… packing a suitcase

Every item you pack should earn its place. A good vector assignment is a tightly packed bag: each vector carries either a real dimension you know or a real joint motion you need to find — nothing redundant. A bad assignment is the over-packed bag full of “just in case” items: extra vectors whose unknown angles correspond to no physical motion, so they bloat the equations without helping you close the loop.

Check 1 — knowns must be dimensions

If a vector has a known magnitude or a known direction, that known should correspond to an actual dimension of the mechanism — a fixed link length, or the fixed orientation of the ground link.

Check 2 — unknowns must be joint variables

If a vector has an unknown magnitude or direction, that unknown should correspond to a joint variable (like a slider displacement) or should define the orientation of a link (like a pin angle). An unknown that means nothing physical is a sign the vector is wrong.

The loop-counting test (do you have enough good loops?)

Once vectors are assigned, confirm the loops are sufficient by asking two questions:

  • Does the number of unknown magnitudes and directions exceed the number of equations by exactly the number of degrees of freedom?
  • Do the known and unknown magnitudes/directions satisfy Check 1 and Check 2 above?

If both are true, you have assigned good vectors and enough loops. If the first fails, you need another independent loop (or you have a miscount); if the second fails, a vector is mis-assigned.

In the real world… an excavator that suddenly won’t solve

Engineers building a hydraulic-excavator simulator routinely hit the loop-counting test in practice: the model freezes or the solver reports too many unknowns. Nine times out of ten the cause is a missing loop — a closing link (a tie-rod, a bucket-linkage strut) was drawn but never given its own vector loop, so the bookkeeping is short one set of equations. Adding the missing independent loop, or deleting a phantom vector that mapped to no real joint, makes the count balance and the machine move. The test isn’t academic; it’s the first thing you check when an assembly model misbehaves.

In the real world… where these joints live

You meet all three of these joints before lunch. The straight sliding joint is a desk drawer or a hydraulic cylinder: it moves along a fixed line, so its direction is known and only its travel is unknown. The circular sliding joint is a curtain rod with a curved track, or a roller coaster car on its rail: fixed radius, variable angle. The contacting circular bodies are a pair of meshing gears or a wheel rolling on a drum: the center-to-center distance is locked at the sum of the radii. Naming the joint correctly is what tells you which quantity is a known dimension and which is the real unknown.

The tell-tale sign of a bad assignment

In every “incorrect” figure, the extra vectors (r̄₅, r̄₆, r̄₇…) carry unknowns that do not map to any joint variable or link orientation. They inflate the unknown count without adding real constraints, so the loop-counting test fails: you appear to need more equations than the mechanism’s DOF can justify. Fewer, physically meaningful vectors always win.

Section 2.3

Closed-Form Solutions to the Position Equations

Closed-form solution. A solution in which the unknowns can be calculated directly — with a finite formula — without any iterative or numerical search.

Closed-form solutions are generally limited to systems of linear equations. But position equations are typically nonlinear: they contain sines and cosines of the unknown angles. So most mechanisms do not have a closed-form position solution. A fortunate few do — notably the four-bar and the crank-slider — and it is worth knowing them because they are everywhere and because the closed form is exact and fast.

Think of it like… a recipe vs. tasting as you go

A closed-form solution is a recipe with exact measurements: combine the numbers in the given formula and you get the answer in one pass, every time. A numerical solution (next section) is tasting and adjusting — add a little, taste, add a little more — you converge on the right flavor but only by repeating the step. When a clean recipe exists, you use it; the crank-slider and four-bar are two dishes that happen to have one.

The offset crank-slider

An engine’s piston mechanism. Crank r₂ rotates; coupler r₃ connects to a slider constrained to a horizontal line offset by a fixed distance. The loop gives two scalar equations. Because the slider runs along a known direction, the angle θ₃ can be isolated and solved with an inverse sine — a genuine closed form.

r₂cosθ₂ + r₃cosθ₃ = r₄ (2.7)
r₂sinθ₂ + r₃sinθ₃ = e (2.8)

Here r₄ is the (unknown) slider position and e the fixed offset. From (2.8), θ₃ = arcsin((e − r₂sinθ₂)/r₃); then (2.7) gives r₄ directly.

Why the four-bar still closes in closed form

Although both unknown angles appear inside trig functions, you can eliminate one angle using a clever substitution (the tangent half-angle identity, or a freudenstein-style rearrangement). The result is a single equation in one unknown that is quadratic in tan(θ/2) — solvable by the quadratic formula. The two roots are exactly the open and crossed branches you toggled in the Section 2.1 tool.

Think of it like… solving a maze by sealing off one corridor

Two coupled unknowns are like two intertwined corridors of a maze — hard to walk both at once. The tangent half-angle substitution is the trick of temporarily walling off one corridor: you rewrite both sinθ and cosθ in terms of a single new variable t = tan(θ/2), so one angle vanishes from the equations entirely. What’s left is one clean path — an ordinary quadratic — that you can walk straight to the exit. Once you know t, you reopen the sealed corridor and recover the second angle for free.

Closed-form crank-slider

Solve a crank-slider exactly

Drive the crank angle θ₂. The tool evaluates the closed-form solution — arcsin for θ₃, then a direct expression for the slider position r₄ — with no iteration. Watch the piston track the crank, and read the exact values in the panel.

When does a closed form even exist?

Rule of thumb: if a single loop produces two equations with two unknown angles and the mechanism is one of the classic single-loop linkages (four-bar, slider-crank, inverted slider-crank), an exact solution can usually be wrung out by hand. Add a second loop — a six-bar, the front loader in Example 2.7 — and the algebra usually defeats hand methods. That is the cue to switch to the numerical approach of Section 2.4.

In the real world… elbow-up or elbow-down

The two roots of the closed-form solution — the open and crossed branches — are not a math curiosity. A robot arm reaching the same point on a table can do it “elbow-up” or “elbow-down”; both place the gripper identically but fold the linkage differently. Controllers must pick a branch and stay on it, because snapping from one to the other mid-task would swing the arm wildly. Your foldable laptop hinge and a desk lamp’s parallel arm face the same choice every time they open.

Section 2.4

Numerical Solutions — Newton’s Method

Most mechanisms have no closed-form position solution. The standard remedy is Newton’s method (also called Newton–Raphson). It converts the original system of nonlinear equations into a sequence of approximate linear systems, each easy to solve, that march toward the true configuration.

The idea in one dimension first

To solve f(x) = 0, guess x⁰. Replace the curve by its tangent line there, and find where the tangent crosses zero — that is your next, better guess:

xnew = x⁰ − f(x⁰) / f′(x⁰)

Repeat. Each step linearizes the problem at the current guess, solves the easy linear problem, and updates. When the change falls below a tolerance, stop.

Think of it like… sliding down a hill in fog

You want the bottom of a valley but can’t see it. So you feel the slope right under your feet, take a straight step in the downhill direction, then stop and feel the slope again. Each step uses only local information (the tangent), yet the steps carry you to the bottom. Newton’s method does this with equations: it replaces the curved problem with the straight tangent at your current guess, steps to where that line hits zero, and re-feels the slope. Start near the valley and you arrive in a few strides; start on the wrong ridge and you may slide into a different valley — the wrong assembly branch.

The multi-variable version (what the method actually solves)

A mechanism gives a system of position equations f₁ = … = fn = 0 in unknowns x₁…xn. The single derivative f′ is replaced by the Jacobian matrix J of all partial derivatives, evaluated at the current guess x̄⁰:

f̄(x̄) = [ f₁(x̄) … fn(x̄) ]ᵀ (2.57)
A = J |x̄=x̄⁰ ,  J = [ ∂f̄/∂x₁ … ∂f̄/∂xn ] (2.59)
x̄ = −A⁻¹ f̄ |x̄=x̄⁰ + x̄⁰ (2.61)

Equation (2.61) is just the multi-variable form of the tangent-line update: solve the linear system A·Δx̄ = −f̄ for the correction, add it to the current guess, and iterate until |x̄ − x̄⁰| ≤ tolerance.

The algorithm (Figure 2.24)

  1. Guess a solution x̄⁰.
  2. Compute f̄(x̄⁰) from (2.57) and the Jacobian A from (2.59).
  3. Compute the updated x̄ from (2.61).
  4. Is |x̄ − x̄⁰| ≤ tolerance? Yes → print x̄ and stop. No → set x̄⁰ = x̄ and return to step 2.

Two things that can go wrong

Newton’s method converges fast (the error roughly squares each step) when it converges. But a poor initial guess can send it to the wrong branch (open vs. crossed) or make it diverge, and if the Jacobian A is singular at a configuration — a kinematic singularity, where the mechanism momentarily loses or gains a freedom — the update in (2.61) blows up because A⁻¹ does not exist. Choosing a starting guess near the previous time-step’s solution is the usual practical fix.

In the real world… inside your CAD software

Every time you drag a part in SolidWorks, Fusion 360, or a game-physics engine and the rest of the assembly follows, a Newton-style solver is running this exact loop dozens of times per second — reusing the previous frame as its initial guess so it converges in two or three iterations. The singularity warning is real too: a robot wrist that lines its axes up (“gimbal lock”) hits a singular Jacobian, which is why industrial robots are programmed to steer around those poses rather than through them.

Section 2.5

The Motion of Points of Interest

Solving the loop gives you every link’s angle. But analysis often needs the motion of a specific point of interest — a point that is not a joint. The good news: once the configuration is known, locating any such point is a short extra vector calculation, no new loop required.

Think of it like… a passenger on a Ferris wheel

Once you know how far the wheel has turned, you know where any seat is — not just the ones at the top or bottom. The seat rides rigidly on the rim, so its position is the hub position plus a fixed-length spoke turned to the current angle. A coupler point works the same way: it rides rigidly on a link, so once the link’s angle is solved, the point is just “the joint, plus a fixed arm at a fixed offset angle.” No new mechanism analysis — one vector addition.

Why we care about these points

  • Mass centers. Their accelerations drive the inertia forces and torques you will need in the dynamics chapters — to size a motor or estimate bearing loads.
  • Force application points. Where a driving or load force acts relates to mechanical advantage (Chapter 7). A coupler point tracing a path may be the working tip of the whole machine.

How to locate one

Let Q be a point fixed on a link (say the coupler). Add a known vector r̄₅ from a solved joint to Q: its magnitude and the angle φ it makes with the link are dimensions, so they are known. The position of Q is then a vector sum that you already have all the pieces for.

In the real world… why a shaking machine rattles its bolts

The mass center of a fast-moving coupler is the most important point of interest you never see. Track it with these equations, differentiate twice, and its acceleration tells you the inertia force the link throws against its pins every cycle. A high-speed packaging machine or an engine at 6,000 rpm can generate coupler-inertia forces of hundreds of newtons that reverse direction dozens of times a second — this is what loosens fasteners, fatigues brackets, and makes the whole frame buzz. Locating the mass center now (position) is step one; the dynamics chapters turn that into the force that sizes the bolts and bearings.

For a coupler point Q on a crank-slider, with r̄q = r̄₂ + r̄₅ (Example 2.11), the coordinates come out as:

rqx = r₂cosθ₂ + r₅cos(θ₃ + φ) (2.67)
rqy = r₂sinθ₂ + r₅sin(θ₃ + φ) (2.68)

Here r₅ and φ are the known dimensions that fix Q on the coupler relative to r̄₃. After the position problem is solved and θ₂, θ₃ are known, (2.67)–(2.68) give Q immediately. Differentiate them once for the velocity of Q, twice for its acceleration.

In the real world… coupler curves that do useful work

The looping path a coupler point traces is a design tool, not just a pretty curve. Engineers pick the offset (r₅, φ) so the curve does a job: a film-advance mechanism in an old movie projector traces a path that pulls each frame down, holds it still, then retracts. A walking-robot leg linkage traces a flat-bottomed curve so the foot moves level along the ground then lifts cleanly. The windshield-wiper tip, a sewing-machine needle bar, and a bread-mixer beater all follow coupler points engineered with exactly these two equations.

The bridge to the next chapters

Everything here was position only. In Chapter 3 you will differentiate these very equations: r̄q differentiated once gives the velocity of Q, twice gives its acceleration. Those accelerations feed the force and torque analysis of the dynamics chapters. The position problem you just learned to solve is the foundation the rest of the course is built on.

Worked Examples

Examples (from the text)

Reveal each solution only after you have attempted it. Each mirrors a worked example from Chapter 2.

Example 2.2 — Position equations of an offset crank-slider

Problem. Write the position equations of an offset crank-slider whose crank r₂ rotates about a fixed pin, whose coupler is r₃, whose slider runs along a horizontal line at offset e, and whose slider position is r₄.

Loop. One closed loop: crank, then coupler, then back along the slider line and the offset. The vector loop equation decomposes into:

r₂cosθ₂ + r₃cosθ₃ − r₄ = 0 (2.7)
r₂sinθ₂ + r₃sinθ₃ − e = 0 (2.8)

Count. Given θ₂ (the input), the unknowns are θ₃ and r₄ — two unknowns, two equations. Solvable.

Closed form. From (2.8): θ₃ = arcsin((e − r₂sinθ₂)/r₃). Then (2.7) gives r₄ = r₂cosθ₂ + r₃cosθ₃. Exact, no iteration. (The arcsin returns two angle families — the open and crossed branches.)

In the real world

This is the engine under your car’s hood. θ₂ is the crankshaft angle, r₄ is the piston’s position in its bore, and the offset e is the small “piston pin desaxe” manufacturers build in to even out side-thrust on the cylinder wall. Equations (2.7)–(2.8) are literally what an engine designer evaluates to find top-dead-center, stroke length, and how fast the piston reverses — the numbers behind compression ratio and redline.

Example 2.7 — A three-loop front loader; counting DOF from the equations

Problem. The skeleton of a front loader (the machine from Chapter 1, Figure 1.17). Derive its position equations and deduce the number of degrees of freedom.

Loops. It is a three-loop mechanism: loop 1 is links 1-2-3-4, loop 2 is 4-5-6-8, loop 3 is 4-8-7-9. (A fourth loop 4-5-6-7-9 exists but is not independent — it is a consequence of loops 2 and 3.) So there are three vector loop equations:

r̄₁ + r̄₄ − r̄₂ = 0̄ (2.33a)
r̄₅ − r̄₈ − r̄₃ = 0̄ (2.33b)
r̄₈ − r̄₇ − r̄₉ − r̄₁₀ = 0̄ (2.33c)

Choosing the origin at the pin between links 1 and 5 and the X axis along r̄₁ gives six scalar equations (2.34)–(2.39), one X and one Y per loop.

DOF from the count. Six scalar position equations; tally the scalar unknowns. The unknown count exceeds the equation count by exactly the number of degrees of freedom. For this loader that excess works out to 2 — consistent with a machine that needs two independent hydraulic inputs (lift and curl).

Think of it like… the joysticks in the cab

The “2” you computed is something the operator feels directly: a front loader has two independent controls — one to lift the arm, one to curl the bucket. Two degrees of freedom means two joysticks. If the equations had returned 1, a single lever would drive the whole linkage; if 3, you’d need a third control. The algebra and the cab agree, because they’re describing the same machine.

Example 2.11 — Position of a point of interest Q on a coupler

Problem. Take the offset crank-slider of Example 2.2 (one DOF; given θ₂, you can already find θ₃ and r₄). Now a point Q on the coupler is connected to ground through a spring. To describe the spring force as a function of θ₂, you must describe the position of Q as a function of θ₂. Derive the equations for Q.

Loop to Q. The coordinates of Q follow a short vector loop r̄q = r̄₂ + r̄₅, where r̄₅ is a known vector locating Q on the coupler. Its X and Y components are:

rqx = r₂cosθ₂ + r₅cos(θ₃ + φ) (2.67)
rqy = r₂sinθ₂ + r₅sin(θ₃ + φ) (2.68)

Takeaway. r₅ and φ are fixed dimensions locating Q on the coupler relative to r̄₃. After the position problem is solved (θ₂, θ₃ known), (2.67)–(2.68) give Q directly — no new unknowns. A point of interest costs you one extra known vector, not a new loop.

In the real world

The spring on point Q is exactly how a screen-door closer, a laptop hinge, or a spring-loaded toggle clamp is analyzed. The spring force depends on how far Q has moved, and Q’s position depends on the input angle — so (2.67)–(2.68) are the link between “how far is the door open” and “how hard is the spring pulling it shut.” Get those coordinates and you can tune the spring so the door closes firmly but doesn’t slam.

The eight steps

  • 1 attach vectors → closed loop
  • 2 write VLE (Σ = 0̄)
  • 3 origin at a fixed point
  • 4 decompose into X, Y
  • 5 list knowns/unknowns
  • 6 solve positions
  • 7 differentiate → velocities
  • 8 differentiate → accelerations

Movability test

  • unknowns > eqns → movable mechanism
  • excess = number of DOF
  • unknowns ≤ eqns → not a mechanism
  • closed-form 4-bar, crank-slider
  • numerical most others (Newton)

Vector checks

  • known ↔ a dimension of mechanism
  • unknown ↔ joint variable / link angle
  • enough loops? excess unknowns = DOF
  • two branches open & crossed configs
  • singularity J becomes non-invertible

Wrap-Up

What you should now be able to do

  • State the kinematic analysis problem for a one-DOF mechanism and identify what is given and what is found.
  • Attach vectors to a mechanism so they close into a loop, and write the vector loop equation.
  • Decompose a VLE into X and Y position equations and use the movability test to confirm the mechanism is solvable.
  • Apply the two vector checks and the loop-counting test to verify a good assignment.
  • Recognize when a closed-form solution exists (four-bar, crank-slider) and solve it.
  • Run Newton’s method on nonlinear position equations and explain the Jacobian’s role and when it fails (singularities).
  • Locate a point of interest on a link with one extra known vector, and set up its position equations.
