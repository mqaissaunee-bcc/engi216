What is a Blake jaw crusher?

Patented by Eli Whitney Blake in 1858, the Blake jaw crusher is one of the oldest and most widely used machines for primary crushing — the first stage of breaking large run‑of‑mine rock or ore down to a manageable size. Material is squeezed between a stationary fixed jaw and a swing jaw that is pivoted at the top. Rock is fed into the wide opening at the top and the crushed product falls out the narrow gap at the bottom. A heavy flywheel stores energy and smooths the heavily fluctuating crushing load between strokes.

The motion is pure four‑bar kinematics. The rotating eccentric is the crank, the pitman is the coupler, the swing jaw is the rocker, and the frame is the fixed ground link. Because the eccentric is by far the shortest link and sits next to the frame, the linkage is a Grashof crank‑rocker: the crank spins all the way around while the jaw oscillates back and forth.   • Gape — the width of the feed opening at the top; sets the largest rock the crusher will accept.
  • Setting — the discharge gap at the bottom (open‑ and closed‑side). It controls product size.
  • Throw — how far the jaw travels horizontally per stroke. More throw = more crushing per cycle.
  • Nip angle — the angle between the two jaw faces. Kept below ~22–26°, or rock slips upward instead of being gripped (“nipped”) and crushed.

Note: production Blake crushers use a double‑toggle mechanism (two toggle plates). This model abstracts the essential motion into a single crank‑rocker four‑bar so the rotation‑to‑oscillation conversion is easy to see. Schematic, not to scale.
